**1. The Problem: Bitcoin\'s Unfinished Business**

Bitcoin has succeeded beyond most early projections as a store of value,
a settlement network, and a treasury reserve asset. It has not yet
succeeded as everyday money. The gap between what Bitcoin was designed
to be and what it has become is not a technical failure. It is an
interface failure --- a presentation problem that has been solved in the
wrong direction.

The wrong direction is the fiat stablecoin. When someone purchases USDT
or USDC, they send dollars to a centralized issuer, the issuer holds
those dollars in US Treasury bills, and a blockchain token is issued in
return. The payment rail is digital. The reserve asset is sovereign
debt. Bitcoin is absent from the transaction entirely. At \$317 billion
in aggregate supply --- growing by approximately \$100 billion per year
--- the stablecoin market has become one of the largest purchasers of US
government debt on earth. Tether alone holds over \$100 billion in
Treasury bills. This is digital currency infrastructure built on the
same monetary system Bitcoin was designed to replace.

From the perspective of Bitcoin\'s addressable market, every dollar in a
fiat stablecoin is a dollar that did not enter Bitcoin. ARK Invest
formalized this observation in November 2025, reducing its 2030
bull-case Bitcoin price target by \$300,000 --- specifically because
stablecoins are capturing the emerging-market demand ARK had previously
assigned to Bitcoin. Monthly stablecoin transaction volumes approached
\$970 billion by August 2025. In Latin America, 71% of stablecoin
activity is tied to cross-border payments. In sub-Saharan Africa and
Southeast Asia, stablecoins are functioning as dollar-access
infrastructure for populations with limited banking services.

These are the populations that should be using Bitcoin. They are using
dollar-denominated tokens instead. The stablecoin market is not taking
market share from Bitcoin in some abstract sense. It is capturing the
specific use case --- peer-to-peer digital payments for the unbanked and
underbanked --- that Satoshi Nakamoto described in the first sentence of
the Bitcoin white paper.

> ***The stablecoin market has not stolen Bitcoin\'s future. It has
> temporarily borrowed it --- and it can be reclaimed by any instrument
> that solves the same problem using Bitcoin as the reserve asset.***

The critical insight is that stablecoin demand is not inherently
competitive with Bitcoin. It is a design choice. Fiat stablecoins are
competitive with Bitcoin because their reserve assets are Treasuries,
not Bitcoin. If the reserve asset changed --- if the dollars flowing
into stablecoins were used to purchase Bitcoin instead of Treasury bills
--- the demand vector would invert entirely. The stablecoin market would
become the largest sustained source of Bitcoin demand in history.

That inversion requires a Bitcoin-backed stablecoin that solves the
problem fiat stablecoins solve: the volatility of a reserve asset that
moves 5--10% in a single week. Bitcoin\'s spot price volatility has
prevented it from functioning as a unit of account for commerce or
savings. Any stablecoin denominated directly in BTC inherits that
volatility and fails the basic stability requirement. This problem has a
solution --- and it begins with separating two functions that every
mature monetary system keeps separate: the unit of account and the
medium of exchange.

share an underlying architecture but serve fundamentally different user
needs and operate with different economic mechanics. Conflating them
obscures both the simplicity of the design and the clarity of the value
proposition for each user type. They are described here separately.

The relationship between the two products is additive and symbiotic. ₿C
is the collateral floor that makes ₿USD\'s $1.00 peg credible through
Bitcoin\'s cyclical volatility --- the slowly moving cumulative average
that caps issuance conservatively. ₿USD is
the circulation layer that gives ₿C real-economy utility --- merchants
price in ₿C, workers receive ₿C-denominated wages, and the denomination\'s
slow appreciation rewards everyone who stays inside the ecosystem.
Neither product is complete without the other.
Together they constitute the first full monetary system --- unit of
account and medium of exchange --- built entirely on Bitcoin\'s reserve
asset.

**3. The Foundation: BTCADP**

Both products depend on a reference price that no single institution
controls. The Bitcoin Average Daily Price (BTCADP) is an open-source
specification for computing a daily Bitcoin reference price from trade
data across qualifying exchanges. It uses per-exchange volume-weighted
average prices aggregated via a 25% trimmed mean with equal exchange
weighting, covering the UTC midnight-to-midnight window. Algorithmic
filters --- minimum trade count, time coverage, bid-ask spread
threshold, and price coherence --- qualify exchanges. The full
specification is published at btcadp.org.

The design principle is institutional independence: the methodology is
the authority, not the publisher. Any party with the specification and
trade data can independently reproduce the number. No institution owns
it. It cannot be captured or discontinued. If the original publisher
ceases to exist, any party can continue computing the BTCADP using the
specification alone.

This matters because the ₿C denomination --- and therefore ₿USD\'s peg
--- rests on this price series. A denomination anchored to a price
computed by a single institution with a financial interest in the
outcome is not credible. A denomination anchored to an open, verifiable,
independently reproducible standard is.

The most consequential property of the ₿C/₿USD ecosystem is not its
denomination mathematics, its reserve architecture, or the stability of
either product. It is the demand flywheel that activates when the system
reaches scale. Each component reinforces the others, and the cycle has
no natural ceiling as long as Bitcoin\'s price trend continues.

This flywheel is structurally different from every other stablecoin
flywheel in existence. Tether\'s growth benefits Tether\'s shareholders
and the US Treasury. Circle\'s growth benefits Circle and the US
government debt market. The ₿C/₿USD flywheel benefits Bitcoin directly
--- because every marginal token issued requires a marginal Bitcoin
purchase. The stablecoin market\'s growth, currently among the fastest
in digital assets, becomes a Bitcoin demand driver rather than a drag.

The virtuous cycle extends beyond reserve dynamics into the macro
structure of Bitcoin adoption. As the ecosystem matures and more
participants earn, save, and spend entirely in ₿C --- never converting
back to fiat --- the proportion exiting to fiat declines. The fiat
boundaries of the system shrink. The reserve system\'s exposure to spot
price risk falls even as the circulating supply grows. Scale makes the
system safer. This is the structural inverse of traditional
fractional-reserve banking, which becomes more fragile as it grows.

**5. The Exit Structure: Incentivizing the Bitcoin Economy**

The exit structure of both products is designed around a single
governing principle: to incentivize holders to remain inside the Bitcoin
ecosystem rather than exit to fiat. This is not a restriction. It is an
alignment of incentives --- reflecting the actual economic cost each
exit type imposes on the reserve system and on the ecosystem as a whole.

The friction on any given exit path is proportional to what that exit
costs Bitcoin. An exit that keeps capital in the ecosystem has low or no
cost. An exit that forces Bitcoin to be sold from reserves and returned
to the traditional financial system carries the full cost of that sale.
The fee structure makes these costs visible and routes capital
accordingly.

The practical consequence of this structure is a natural routing of
capital. When a ₿C holder wants to spend, they convert to ₿USD --- the

wants to price or denominate holdings in ₿C --- also near-frictionless. When

Over time, as the ecosystem deepens and more daily economic activity is
denominated in ₿C --- payroll, invoicing, merchant pricing --- the
incentive to exit to fiat diminishes naturally. The ecosystem
becomes self-sustaining not through restrictions but through the
superior economic properties of staying inside it. A denomination that
appreciates slowly and predictably is a better foundation for economic

government debt. The fee structure accelerates this dynamic; it does not
create it.

Specific holding periods, tiered fee schedules, and minimum balance

and product design. The structural principle --- that exit friction
should be proportional to the ecosystem cost of that exit --- is
invariant.

**6. The Treasury Company Opportunity: Monetizing Existing Risk**

Bitcoin treasury companies are not being asked to take a new risk. They
have already taken the risk. Every company in this cohort has committed
its balance sheet to the thesis that Bitcoin will appreciate over time.
The ₿C/₿USD architecture does not add to that risk. It creates
revenue-generating services on top of an existing risk position.

Consider the ₿USD product. A treasury company offering ₿USD is, in
economic substance, doing what the most sophisticated Bitcoin-focused
public companies already do --- holding Bitcoin and offering exposure
to its appreciation through a structured financial product. The
₿USD product extends this to retail and institutional participants directly,
without equity dilution, without leverage, and with a denomination that
smooths the experience of holding a volatile underlying asset. The
Ledger 2 backstop commitment is structurally equivalent to the risk
already carried on the balance sheet. What changes is that the risk now
has a fee stream attached.

**Revenue Streams**

**①** Issuance fees on ₿C and ₿USD. A fee charged when fiat enters the
system. At 0.5% on a \$10 billion combined circulating supply with 20%
annual turnover, this generates \$100 million per year in fee income. At
\$100 billion in supply, the figure exceeds \$1 billion annually.

**②** Redemption and conversion fees, tiered by exit path. Near-zero for
₿C-to-₿USD conversions that stay in the ecosystem. Moderate for
₿C-to-BTC exits. Highest for fiat redemptions. The fee structure is the
primary mechanism routing capital into the lower-friction paths --- and
generating predictable recurring revenue at every tier.

**③** The Bitcoin appreciation spread. The ₿C denomination appreciates
more slowly than Bitcoin\'s spot price by design --- it is a cumulative
average of 16 years of price history. Treasury companies hold Bitcoin as
their reserve asset. The spread between Bitcoin\'s spot appreciation and
the ₿C denomination\'s gradual upward drift accrues to the consortium.
This is not a fee. It is the natural consequence of holding a
faster-appreciating asset against a slower-appreciating obligation. On a
\$10 billion backstop position, a 60-percentage-point differential over
five years generates \$6 billion of surplus value --- without a single
fee being charged.

**④** The equity re-rating premium. A Bitcoin treasury company that also
operates the dominant bridge denomination and the primary
Bitcoin-backed medium of exchange is not comparable to a company that
simply holds Bitcoin on its balance sheet. The former has recurring
revenue, a growing user base, a defensible moat in a \$300+ billion
industry, and structural alignment between its industry\'s growth and
its primary asset\'s appreciation. Markets assign different multiples to
these business models. The transition is a re-rating event.

**The Treasury Company as the New Central Bank**

The analogy is precise and worth stating directly. Central banks perform
three functions: they issue the currency their economy uses, they hold
the reserve assets that back it, and they set the monetary policy that
governs it. Bitcoin treasury companies, acting as a consortium, perform
two of these functions --- issuance and reserve custody --- while the
third is handled by arithmetic rather than a committee.

This is an improvement, not a limitation. The two most consequential
failure modes of central bank monetary systems are discretionary supply
expansion and discretionary policy. Both require human judgment, and
both have produced consistent long-run outcomes: purchasing power
erosion for currency holders and periodic crises when the judgment
proves incorrect.

The consortium eliminates both failure modes. Bitcoin\'s supply is fixed
at 21 million coins by protocol. No consortium member can create more
Bitcoin to expand reserves. The ₿C denomination is determined by the
arithmetic mean of historical prices --- an output of the data, not a
policy input. No committee sets the ₿C rate. These constraints are not
restrictions on the consortium\'s freedom. They are the source of its
credibility.

A central bank\'s credibility rests on the perceived probability that it
will honor its commitments. That credibility is fragile --- earned over
decades and destroyable in days by a single policy decision under
political pressure. The consortium\'s credibility rests on the
verifiability of its reserves and the transparency of its methodology.
Anyone can check the on-chain wallets. Anyone can reproduce the BTCADP
calculation. Anyone can verify the ₿C denominator in real time. The
credibility is structural, not reputational.

> ***The consortium does not promise to maintain the value of the
> currency. The mathematics does. That distinction is the entire
> argument.***

**9. Timing: Why the Window Is Now**

Two conditions had to develop independently before the ₿C/₿USD model
became viable. Both have now been met simultaneously for the first time.

The first is the ₿C denomination\'s stability. The mathematical property
that makes ₿C useful for commerce and pricing --- its insensitivity to
any single day\'s price movement --- is a function of how many days of
price history the cumulative average incorporates. In 2011, with roughly
365 days of history, a single day\'s price could shift ₿C by 0.3%. As of
early 2026, with over 6,200 days, the sensitivity is below 0.01%. There
is no shortcut to this accumulation. It is the product of time, and that
time has now elapsed. The stability improves with every passing day ---
time itself is a structural tailwind for the denomination.

The second is the existence of a Bitcoin treasury company cohort with
sufficient balance sheet scale to backstop a meaningful stablecoin. The first significant corporate Bitcoin treasury
allocation was announced in August 2020. The cohort of publicly traded
companies holding Bitcoin as a primary reserve asset at institutional
scale is a product of the five years since. The combined Bitcoin
holdings of public treasury companies now exceed one million BTC. This
reserve base, organized into a consortium, would constitute one of the
largest Bitcoin custodians in existence.

The third dimension of timing is competitive. CBDC development is
accelerating globally. Over 130 countries are in some stage of central
bank digital currency exploration or rollout. The window in which a
Bitcoin-backed alternative to CBDC payment infrastructure can be
established --- before sovereign digital currencies become the default
infrastructure for retail payments --- is open now and will not remain
open indefinitely. The ₿C/₿USD architecture does not require regulatory
approval to begin operating. It requires coordination among a small
number of institutions that have already demonstrated their commitment
to Bitcoin and their capacity to operate at institutional scale.

**10. Risk Analysis**

**Bitcoin Price Decline**

A sustained, severe Bitcoin price decline that depresses spot below the
average acquisition price of reserves is the most acute risk to the
system. In this scenario, users seeking fiat redemption would receive
fewer BTC per token than were originally deposited, requiring Ledger 2
to cover the shortfall.

The structural mitigation is the overcollateralization of Ledger 2,
drawn from the treasury companies\' existing Bitcoin holdings. The ₿C
denomination\'s stability also reduces the correlation between spot
price declines and redemption pressure --- because the value a ₿C or
₿USD holder sees in their wallet does not change materially when spot
moves 10%. Users who entered the system for ₿C\'s stability see no
reason to exit during a spot downturn. And the tiered exit structure
imposes the highest friction on fiat exits --- precisely the exits that
would stress reserves during a decline.

The critical framing: each consortium member already carries this risk
on their balance sheet. The Ledger 2 backstop commitment does not add to
their Bitcoin price exposure. It formalizes an existing exposure with a
defined perimeter and attaches a revenue stream to it.

**Coordinated Attack: Short Plus Mass Redemption**

A sophisticated attack involves simultaneously shorting Bitcoin at scale
while triggering mass ₿USD redemptions --- using forced reserve selling
to amplify the short position\'s profitability. The structural defenses
are several: redemption fee friction raises the cost of coordinated mass
exits; the ₿C denomination\'s stability prevents panic-driven
redemptions triggered by spot moves; the two-ledger reserve structure
provides a Ledger 2 backstop before Ledger 1 is stressed; and the
transparency of on-chain reserves removes the information asymmetry that
amplifies traditional bank runs --- any observer can verify reserve
adequacy in real time.

A sufficiently capitalized and determined attack, combined with a
genuinely distressed reserve position, could create meaningful stress.
This risk is not zero, and it is not unique to this system --- it is the
risk every leveraged financial institution faces, managed here with
structural tools rather than regulatory backstops.

**Regulatory Uncertainty**

Stablecoin regulation is evolving. The regulatory treatment of a
Bitcoin-backed stablecoin --- pegged to $1.00 USD but collateralized
by Bitcoin rather than fiat reserves --- remains untested in most
jurisdictions. The structural mitigation is the
consortium\'s distributed jurisdiction across member companies --- no
single regulatory action shuts down the entire system. The ₿C
denomination itself requires no permission to compute or use. It is an
open mathematical standard that any party can calculate regardless of
regulatory status. The denomination outlasts any regulatory action
against the tokens that use it.

**Consortium Coordination**

Multiple independent publicly traded companies must agree on reserve
management, fee structures, and operational standards. The foundational
methodology is independent of the consortium --- the BTCADP
specification is published, the ₿C formula is arithmetic, and reserve
management rules can be codified in a charter that does not require
active committee decisions for routine operations. Governance risk is an
organizational challenge, not a structural flaw in the architecture.

**11. The Asymmetric Upside**

The downside scenario for a consortium member is bounded: the backstop
commitment formalizes existing Bitcoin price risk with defined
parameters and attaches a fee stream to it. The upside is genuinely
asymmetric.

At \$10 billion in combined ₿C and ₿USD supply --- approximately 3% of
USDT\'s current market cap --- the consortium is generating meaningful
recurring fee revenue, holding an appreciating Bitcoin backstop, and
operating the only circulating digital currency
provably backed by a scarce reserve asset on-chain.

At \$100 billion in combined supply, the consortium is the
second-largest stablecoin issuer in the world. The Bitcoin reserve
requirement at that scale --- approximately 1.2 million BTC at current
prices --- has absorbed demand equivalent to the entire institutional
ETF inflow of the past two years. The fee revenue at moderate annual
turnover exceeds \$500 million. The spread between Bitcoin\'s
appreciation and the ₿C denomination\'s slower drift has generated
surplus value across the entire backstop position.

At \$500 billion --- a third of the projected 2030 market --- the
consortium holds reserves comparable to many sovereign central banks.
The ₿USD is the globally recognized Bitcoin-backed stablecoin. The ₿C
denomination is a unit of account for merchants, payroll systems, and
financial products across dozens of countries. The consortium\'s member
companies are, by any functional definition, operating as central banks
of the digital Bitcoin economy --- without discretionary money printing,
without political pressure, and without the inflation mandate that has
eroded the purchasing power of every fiat currency in modern history.

+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+
| **The asymmetry, stated plainly:**                                    |
|                                                                       |
| If ₿C and ₿USD succeed at 10% market share, consortium members have   |
| built the largest Bitcoin-backed monetary institution in history and  |
| a multi-hundred-billion-dollar financial infrastructure business on   |
| top of a position they already hold.                                  |
|                                                                       |
| *If the system does not reach scale, the downside is the cost of      |
| coordination and development --- against an existing Bitcoin position |
| that was already the base case.*                                      |
+-----------------------------------------------------------------------+

There is one final dimension worth naming. The companies that do not
participate in this consortium may, in time, be transacting using the
monetary infrastructure built by the companies that did. The stablecoin
and digital currency markets will grow to multiple trillions of dollars
with or without a Bitcoin-backed alternative. If the Bitcoin treasury
company cohort does not build that alternative, Tether, Circle, and
eventually central bank digital currencies will continue to capture the
capital flows that Bitcoin was designed to absorb. The choice is not
between building this and doing nothing. It is between building this and
watching the stablecoin market grow on US Treasury collateral for the
next decade.

**Conclusion**

Bitcoin solved the problem of digital scarcity. It defined a monetary
network with a fixed supply, a transparent ledger, and rules enforced by
mathematics rather than institutions. What it did not solve was the
presentation problem --- how to offer the stability that commerce
requires without abandoning the reserve asset that makes
Bitcoin\'s monetary properties unique.

The ₿C/₿USD ecosystem solves the presentation problem with two products
that serve distinct purposes and reinforce each other. ₿C provides the
unit of account: a pricing denomination whose stability derives from the
mathematical properties of 16 years of accumulated price history, and
whose value grows as long as Bitcoin\'s long-run price trend continues.
₿USD provides the medium of exchange: a transactional stablecoin that
circulates on Bitcoin\'s sidechain infrastructure, redeemable for
Bitcoin reserves held transparently on the base layer.

The exit structure ties both products together under the principle that
matters most: capital should be incentivized to stay inside the Bitcoin
economy. Near-frictionless conversion between ₿C and ₿USD keeps
participants in the ecosystem. Moderate fees on BTC exits keep
capital in Bitcoin\'s orbit. High fees on fiat exits reflect the real
cost of that exit --- Bitcoin sold from reserves --- and route capital
accordingly. The result is an ecosystem that becomes more
self-sustaining as it grows, because the friction on leaving exceeds the
incentive to do so.

The components exist. The specification is published. The treasury
companies that would form the consortium already hold the necessary
reserves. The mathematical conditions that make ₿C stable enough for
commerce have been met by the accumulation of over 6,200 days of price
history. The sidechain infrastructure for token circulation exists or is
within the consortium\'s capacity to build.

What remains is the recognition that the stablecoin market is not a
threat to Bitcoin\'s future. It is the largest unsolved Bitcoin adoption
problem in existence. And the only entities positioned to solve it are
the ones that have already committed their balance sheets to Bitcoin\'s
long-run success.

> ***The central banks of the digital Bitcoin economy have not yet been
> built. The institutions that build them will define how value moves
> globally for the next century.***
