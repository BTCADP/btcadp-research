**COULD BITCOIN TREASURY COMPANIES**

**OFFER A VIABLE ALTERNATIVE TO**

**CENTRAL BANK CBDCs?**

btcadp.org • March 2026

Central banks around the world are developing digital currencies. The

Bank of International Settlements estimates that over 130 countries are

in some stage of CBDC exploration or rollout, with several \-\-- China,

the Bahamas, Nigeria \-\-- already operating live systems. The case for

CBDCs is made in the language of efficiency: faster payments, lower

costs, financial inclusion for the unbanked. But efficiency is not the

only thing a monetary system delivers to those who use it. It also

delivers, or fails to deliver, financial privacy, protection from

arbitrary account action, and freedom from the coercive use of monetary

policy as a behavioral instrument.

What would it mean to build a digital currency that preserves the

freedom properties of physical cash while providing the settlement

efficiency of a digital network \-\-- and does so without a central
issuer

capable of inflating the supply, surveilling every transaction, or

weaponizing access? This article examines one concrete proposal: the

Treasury-Backed Digital Currency, or ₿USD, a Bitcoin-backed stablecoin

issued by a consortium of publicly traded Bitcoin treasury companies and

priced in the BTCC (Bitcoin Currency) unit of account.

The ₿USD is not a speculative concept. Its components \-\-- a published

reference price specification, a mathematically defined denomination,

and a class of institutional participants with existing Bitcoin holdings

exceeding 600,000 BTC \-\-- are already in place. What the proposal

requires is their assembly into a functioning monetary institution. What

it offers, against the backdrop of accelerating CBDC development

globally, is a digitally native alternative that does not require

surrendering the financial autonomy that physical cash currently

provides.

**I. What CBDCs Are \-\-- and What They Are Not**

A Central Bank Digital Currency is a liability of the central bank,

denominated in the national unit of account, and accessible directly by

the public in digital form. It is, in effect, a digital banknote \-\--

except that unlike a physical banknote, it is not bearer money. Every

balance and every transaction is recorded on infrastructure controlled

by, or directly accessible to, the issuing authority.

CBDCs come in two main forms: retail (accessible directly by citizens

and businesses) and wholesale (used for interbank settlement). Most

current proposals target the retail layer, aiming to replace or

supplement physical cash. China\\\'s digital yuan \-\-- the e-CNY \-\--
is the

most advanced and most widely deployed example, with hundreds of

millions of wallets distributed and mandatory acceptance at some

government venues. The European Central Bank, the Bank of England, the

Federal Reserve, and most other major central banks are in active

development or pilot stages.

The efficiency case for CBDCs is real and should be acknowledged

clearly. Programmable money that can be transferred in seconds without

passing through commercial bank intermediaries would represent a genuine

improvement over much existing payment infrastructure. The financial

inclusion argument \-\-- that a central bank account could serve

populations currently excluded from commercial banking \-\-- is

substantive. These are not fabricated benefits.

The structural problems are also real, and they are not addressed by any

current CBDC proposal, because they are inherent to the architecture

rather than artifacts of implementation.

**The Inflation Problem Has Not Been Solved**

A CBDC does not alter the fundamental property of central bank money:

that it can be created, expanded, and devalued at the discretion of

monetary authorities. The US dollar has lost approximately 97% of its

purchasing power since the Federal Reserve\\\'s founding in 1913. The
euro

has lost roughly 35% of its purchasing power since its introduction in

1999\. This is not a malfunction \-\-- it is the system operating as

designed. Inflation is the mechanism through which governments finance

expenditures beyond tax revenue. A digital dollar retains this property

in full, and no CBDC proposal has suggested otherwise. The speed of the

payment rail does not change the economics of the currency running on

it.

The mathematics of compounding erosion are worth stating plainly,

because they are rarely presented in the context of CBDC discussions. At

a 2% annual inflation target \-\-- the explicit policy goal of most
major

central banks \-\-- a unit of currency loses half its purchasing power
in

approximately 35 years. At 4% \-\-- which has characterized significant

periods of recent US monetary history \-\-- the same erosion takes 18

years. At 8%, a rate experienced in the US in 2022, purchasing power

halves in 9 years. These are not edge cases. They are the stated

operating parameters of the system. A CBDC holder is holding a balance

that its own issuer is targeting for systematic, indefinite devaluation.

The technical term for this mechanism is seigniorage: the profit a

government earns by issuing currency whose face value exceeds the cost

of its production. In a fiat system, seigniorage is collected not at the

printing press but distributed silently across all holders of the

currency, who find their savings worth progressively less without any

transaction occurring. It is, in economic substance, a tax \-\-- one
that

requires no vote, no legislation, and no visible collection mechanism. A

CBDC does not eliminate seigniorage. It eliminates the last instrument

\-\-- physical cash \-\-- that allows holders to partially opt out of it
by

holding a form of money the central bank cannot directly devalue.

CBDCs may in fact deepen the inflation problem rather than simply

perpetuating it. The programmable capabilities discussed below \-\--

negative interest rates on held balances, expiry dates on unspent

currency \-\-- are proposals specifically designed to eliminate the
floor

that physical cash currently places under monetary debasement. A holder

who faces negative rates on a bank deposit today can withdraw to

physical cash and earn zero rather than negative returns. A CBDC system

that replaces physical cash with a programmable balance removes this

option entirely. The lower bound on interest rates \-\-- already pushed
to

its limits by central banks in the decade following 2008 \-\-- would

effectively be abolished.

**Programmability as a Control Mechanism**

The defining technical property of a CBDC \-\-- and the one that most

distinguishes it from physical cash \-\-- is programmability. Unlike a

physical banknote, which is a bearer instrument that changes hands

without any record or condition, a CBDC balance is software. Software

can have rules. And the rules that have been publicly proposed,

discussed, or quietly implemented in various CBDC programs represent a

meaningful expansion of the state\\\'s capacity to direct economic

behavior.

Expiry dates on currency. The Bank of England, the Bank for

International Settlements, and several academic economists have

discussed time-limited CBDC balances \-\-- money that expires if not
spent

within a defined window. The stated rationale is macroeconomic: to

stimulate spending during downturns by eliminating the option to save.

The practical effect is the elimination of the holder\\\'s ability to

defer consumption, which is one of the foundational functions of money

as a store of value. Physical cash does not expire. A digital currency

that does is a fundamentally different instrument.

Negative interest rates on held balances. In conventional banking, the

existence of physical cash places a theoretical lower bound on interest

rates: depositors will withdraw to cash rather than accept a guaranteed

loss on held deposits. CBDCs eliminate this escape valve. If physical

cash is phased out and replaced by a CBDC, monetary authorities can

impose negative holding rates \-\-- effectively a tax on saving \-\--
with

no mechanism for the holder to avoid it except by spending. Several

economists who have advocated for deeply negative rates have cited the

elimination of cash as a necessary precondition for their

implementation.

Categorical spending restrictions. Programmable money can be issued with

conditions on its use. This has been presented in benign forms:

government benefit payments that can only be spent on food, or stimulus

payments restricted to domestic businesses. The same technical

mechanism, applied by a less benign authority, enables the restriction

of purchasing to approved vendors, categories, or geographic areas.

China\\\'s e-CNY has been distributed with geographic spending limits as

part of regional pilot programs. The infrastructure that enables the

benign version also enables the restrictive one.

Social and political access control. A CBDC account is a permissioned

account. The issuing authority \-\-- or institutions operating under its

regulatory oversight \-\-- decides who can hold one, on what conditions,

and with what ongoing obligations. In February 2022, Canadian

authorities invoked emergency powers to freeze bank accounts of donors

to the trucker convoy protest without individual court orders. Every

account affected was a conventional bank account, subject to the same

access architecture a CBDC would use, but requiring bank cooperation. A

retail CBDC would place the same capability directly in the hands of the

central authority, without requiring commercial bank intermediaries to

comply.

**Total Transactional Surveillance**

Physical cash transactions leave no record. A CBDC transaction

necessarily does. Every payment, every merchant, every amount, every

timestamp \-\-- the complete record of an individual\\\'s economic life
\-\--

is recorded on infrastructure that the issuing authority either operates

directly or can access. This is not a privacy policy question. It is a

structural property of the architecture. You cannot have a central bank

liability with central record-keeping that does not produce a central

record.

China\\\'s e-CNY is instructive. The People\\\'s Bank of China has
described

the system\\\'s privacy model as \\\'controllable anonymity\\\' \-\--
meaning

transactions below certain thresholds appear anonymous to merchants and

other counterparties, but the PBOC retains full access to the underlying

data. This is not anonymity in any meaningful sense. It is pseudonymity

with a government override. Western CBDC proposals have been more

circumspect about describing surveillance capabilities, but the

architecture produces the same result: a comprehensive,

government-accessible ledger of every retail economic transaction in the

economy.

The implications extend beyond the obvious privacy concern.

Comprehensive transaction surveillance enables the identification of

political donations, union activity, religious giving, participation in

disfavored associations, and any other economic behavior that an

authority might choose to monitor or penalize. Whether any particular

government would choose to use this capability is a political question.

That the capability exists and cannot be removed without dismantling the

architecture is a structural one.

**Nigeria\\\'s eNaira and the Cash Withdrawal Floor**

Nigeria\\\'s eNaira, launched in October 2021, provides the most

instructive early example of how CBDC implementation interacts with the

elimination of cash. Adoption was slow initially, with citizens

resistant to the new system. In late 2022, the Central Bank of Nigeria

sharply restricted cash withdrawals, limiting individuals to

approximately 20,000 naira per week (roughly \\\$45 at the time),

effectively coercing adoption of the eNaira by making the alternative

\-\-- physical cash \-\-- practically inaccessible.

The episode is a working demonstration of a pattern that should be

expected wherever CBDCs are introduced alongside policies that restrict

cash access. The CBDC is not directly mandated; it becomes the path of

least resistance when the alternatives are deliberately constrained.

Financial inclusion as a stated goal becomes financial compulsion as a

practical outcome.

**II. The ₿USD: A Counter-Architecture**

The term ₿USD \-\-- Treasury-Backed Digital Currency \-\-- is chosen to
mark

a structural contrast with the CBDC model, not merely a branding

distinction. A ₿USD is issued by a consortium of publicly traded Bitcoin

treasury companies \-\-- firms whose primary business is acquiring and

holding Bitcoin as a reserve asset. As of early 2026, several such

companies exist, including Strategy (formerly MicroStrategy), MARA

Holdings, Metaplanet, Twenty One Capital, and others, with combined

Bitcoin holdings exceeding 600,000 BTC.

Where a CBDC is a new form of an old instrument \-\-- sovereign-issued

money, digitized \-\-- a ₿USD is a genuinely different kind of

institution. Its reserves are a scarce asset no issuer can produce. Its

denomination is determined by arithmetic rather than policy. Its issuers

are private companies, not sovereign authorities. And the properties

that CBDCs eliminate \-\-- transaction privacy, censorship resistance,

freedom from behavioral programming \-\-- are structural features of the

₿USD architecture rather than policy choices that might be reversed.

This section describes how the ₿USD works. The following section

addresses directly how its properties compare to those of a CBDC across

the dimensions that matter most for individual financial freedom.

**Two-Ledger Reserve System**

A ₿USD is issued against Bitcoin reserves held on Bitcoin\\\'s base
layer.

When a customer purchases a ₿USD token, they send fiat currency to a

consortium member. The treasury company uses those funds to purchase

Bitcoin at the current spot price and deposits the acquired Bitcoin into

Ledger 1, the issuance pool. One ₿USD token is issued to the customer.

At the moment of issuance, the reserves in Ledger 1 perfectly back the

outstanding token.

Ledger 2 \-\-- the reserve backstop \-\-- holds Bitcoin drawn from the

treasury company\\\'s existing holdings. This ledger guarantees

redemptions when Bitcoin\\\'s spot price has declined and Ledger 1\\\'s

holdings are insufficient to cover the full BTCC face value. Both

ledgers are held in publicly addressable wallets on Bitcoin\\\'s base

layer, verifiable by any observer in real time. The reserves are not

lent, staked, or rehypothecated. They are sold only when a customer

exits the ₿USD ecosystem by redeeming a token for fiat\-\--from Ledger 1

first, and from Ledger 2 when spot price declines leave Ledger 1

insufficient. Tokens circulating within the ecosystem\-\--moving between

wallets as a medium of exchange\-\--create no reserve pressure at any
BTC

spot price. As the ecosystem matures and more commerce occurs natively

in BTCC, the proportion of tokens ever redeemed for fiat naturally

declines. Between minting and the event of fiat exit, the Bitcoin backs

the issued tokens and does nothing else.

**The BTCC Denomination: Stability Without a Peg**

₿USD tokens are pegged to the US dollar, with the ecosystem priced in BTCC (Bitcoin Currency) \-\-- defined as

the arithmetic mean of every historical Bitcoin Average Daily Price

(BTCADP) from the genesis block on January 3, 2009 through the previous

completed day. As of early 2026, this cumulative average incorporates

over 6,200 daily observations and sits at approximately \\\$18,000.

The stability property follows directly from how cumulative averages

work. With N exceeding 6,200, a day on which Bitcoin\\\'s spot price
moves

10% shifts the BTCC price by less than 0.01%. A 40% single-day crash

shifts the BTCC price by approximately 0.04%. These are arithmetic

facts, not policy commitments. The BTCC denomination cannot be inflated,

cannot have an expiry date applied to it, and cannot be programmed with

spending conditions. It is a number produced by a public calculation

from public data. No institution controls it.

The BTCADP reference price that feeds the BTCC average is computed daily

using a published open-source specification. Any party with the

specification and underlying trade data can independently reproduce the

number. The methodology is the authority. No central bank, no company,

and no government\\\'s continued operation is required for the

denomination to remain valid and computable.

The most consequential row is transaction surveillance. Physical cash

cash: one is a liability of an institution, and one is a direct holding.

The most consequential difference is the monetary policy row. A ₿USD

consortium cannot inflate the money supply. This is not a governance

commitment that could be reversed under political pressure \-\-- it is a

physical property of the reserve asset. No act of the consortium, no

governance vote, no emergency measure can produce additional Bitcoin. To

issue more ₿USD tokens, the consortium must acquire more Bitcoin on the

open market at market prices. The money supply is constrained by the

finite supply of Bitcoin and the consortium\\\'s ability and willingness

to purchase it.

This hard constraint eliminates the inflationary monetary policy that

has characterized every fiat currency in modern history. Consider what

the BTCC denomination actually represents: the arithmetic mean of every

Bitcoin price observation since January 2009, updated daily. Because

Bitcoin\\\'s long-run price trend has been upward, the cumulative
average

rises over time \-\-- slowly, smoothly, and predictably. The BTCC

fiat-equivalent price in January 2024 was higher than in January 2023.

It was higher still in January 2025. A holder who denominated their

holdings in BTCC three years ago holds more fiat purchasing power today

than when they started, without any investment activity whatsoever. The

denomination itself did the work.

Compare this directly to the CBDC experience. A holder of a digital

dollar in January 2021 who simply retained that balance through the end

of 2023 lost approximately 19% of their purchasing power to cumulative

inflation over that period \-\-- with no policy failure, no crisis, and
no

discretionary action beyond what the Federal Reserve openly announced as

its target. The loss was policy. For the CBDC holder, inflation is not a

risk to be managed. It is a certainty to be accepted.

**IV. How Token Circulation Preserves Base Layer Integrity**

₿USD tokens circulate as issued assets on a Bitcoin sidechain \-\--
either

Liquid, an existing federated Bitcoin sidechain, or a purpose-built

consortium-operated alternative. When one user pays another, the token

moves from one sidechain address to another. No Bitcoin moves. The

reserves in Ledger 1 and Ledger 2 sit untouched on the base layer. A

consumer paying for coffee does not interact with Bitcoin\\\'s base
layer.

A worker receiving a salary in ₿USD does not interact with Bitcoin\\\'s

base layer. The base layer secures the reserves. The sidechain handles

commerce.

This separation has a freedom-relevant implication. Routine commerce on

the sidechain occurs without creating base-layer transaction records. A

million ₿USD payments can occur in a day without a single satoshi moving

on-chain. The only moments that interact with Bitcoin\\\'s base layer
are

minting (when new tokens are created against Bitcoin purchases) and fiat

redemption (when tokens are burned and Bitcoin is sold to return fiat).

Between those endpoints, circulation is a sidechain matter \-\--

pseudonymous, fast, and architecturally separate from the
consortium\\\'s

reserve management.

Compare this to a CBDC, where every transaction \-\-- including every

routine commercial payment \-\-- is recorded by infrastructure the
issuer

controls. The CBDC\\\'s efficiency and the CBDC\\\'s surveillance are
the

same feature: a centralized record of every economic transaction. The

₿USD\\\'s separation of token circulation from reserve settlement is not

merely an architectural choice for scalability. It is the mechanism by

which routine commercial privacy is preserved.

A comment on the distinction from paper gold is also relevant here.

Financial products built on top of scarce assets frequently suppress the

underlying asset\\\'s price by creating synthetic exposure that absorbs

demand without requiring physical acquisition. Paper gold \-\-- futures,

ETF shares, derivatives \-\-- allows investors to gain price exposure

without buying metal, diverting demand away from the spot market. Every

₿USD token in circulation is backed by real Bitcoin purchased at spot at

the moment of minting. Sidechain circulation is neutral with respect to

Bitcoin\\\'s spot price because the Bitcoin is already purchased. Every

new token minted requires fresh Bitcoin acquisition. The financial

product amplifies demand for its reserve asset rather than suppressing

it.

**V. The Economics for Each Party**

**What the Customer Receives**

The customer who holds ₿USD tokens trades Bitcoin\\\'s spot volatility
for

the BTCC denomination\\\'s stability. But the transaction is better than
a

simple fiat stablecoin trade in two respects. First, the stability is

genuine: the BTCC denomination changes by fractions of a percent per

day, regardless of what Bitcoin\\\'s spot price does, as an arithmetic

property of a 6,200-day cumulative average. Second, the denomination

trends in the direction of appreciation rather than erosion. As long as

Bitcoin\\\'s spot price remains above its historical cumulative average,

the BTCC fiat-equivalent price rises slowly. A ₿USD holder holds a

currency whose purchasing power improves gradually over time, the

inverse of the quiet erosion experienced by holders of dollar-pegged

stablecoins or CBDC balances.

And critically, the customer holds this stability without surrendering

financial privacy to an issuing authority, without accepting the

possibility of programmable spending restrictions, and without holding a

balance that can be frozen by administrative command. The stability is

delivered without the surveillance.

**What the Treasury Company Receives**

The treasury company accepts the downside risk: it must honor

redemptions at the BTCC fiat-equivalent price even when Bitcoin\\\'s
spot

price has declined and Ledger 1\\\'s holdings are insufficient. In

exchange, it captures the spread between Bitcoin\\\'s spot appreciation

rate and the BTCC denomination\\\'s much slower appreciation rate.

This spread accelerates non-linearly as Bitcoin\\\'s price rises. A

treasury company that issues 1,000 ₿USD tokens when Bitcoin\\\'s spot

price is \\\$70,000 and the BTCC price is \\\$18,000 receives \\\$18
million

in fiat and acquires approximately 257 BTC for Ledger 1. If Bitcoin

subsequently rises to \\\$140,000, Ledger 1 holds Bitcoin worth \\\$36

million against a BTCC liability of approximately \\\$18 million. The

surplus equals the full original intake. At \\\$200,000 Bitcoin, the

surplus exceeds \\\$33 million \-\-- 186% above the return from simple

holding. These are not linear returns. The profit accelerates because

the liability \-\-- the BTCC denomination \-\-- barely moves while the
asset

tracks spot directly.

Transaction fees on minting and fiat redemption \-\-- typically 0.1% to

0.5% \-\-- provide additional revenue independent of Bitcoin\\\'s price

direction. At meaningful circulation scale, even modest fees are

substantial.

**VI. Distributed Issuance vs. Sovereign Authority**

A central bank is a single institution. Its failure \-\-- through

political capture, catastrophic policy error, infrastructure compromise,

or loss of public confidence \-\-- is a systemic event with no internal

fallback. The risks are not hypothetical. Argentina\\\'s repeated peso

crises, Turkey\\\'s 2021-2022 lira collapse (the currency lost over 40%
of

its value in a matter of weeks), and Venezuela\\\'s bolivar
hyperinflation

are recent examples of sovereign monetary authority failures that

destroyed the savings of millions of people holding the relevant

currency. In each case, holders had no recourse and no alternative

denomination. The currency had no other issuer.

The ₿USD consortium model distributes issuance risk across multiple

independent publicly traded companies, each in its own regulatory

jurisdiction, each maintaining its own reserve ledgers. If one

consortium member fails, is seized by regulators, or becomes insolvent,

the others continue operating. The tokens are not cancelled. The

denomination remains valid and computable.

More fundamentally, the BTCC denomination \-\-- the unit of account on

which all ₿USD tokens are based \-\-- is independent of every consortium

member. It requires no company\\\'s continued existence to be
computable.

The specification is published and open-source. Any party with access to

Bitcoin exchange trade data can independently compute the daily BTCADP

and the cumulative BTCC price. The denomination survives the consortium.

An institution-independent unit of account is not a feature available in

any CBDC, because a CBDC\\\'s unit of account is the national currency,

which by definition depends on the continued operation of the sovereign

authority that issues it.

  *\*A CBDC issuer that fails takes its currency with it. The BTCC*

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  *denomination requires no issuer at all \-\-- only the specification,
  the*

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  *trade data, and the arithmetic. It cannot be shut down because there*

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  *is nothing to shut down.\**

**VII. Risks and Limitations**

The ₿USD model has genuine structural risks that warrant clear

statement.

**Sustained Bitcoin Price Decline**

The most significant operational risk is a prolonged decline in

Bitcoin\\\'s spot price that underfunds Ledger 1 and forces draws on

Ledger 2 when tokens are redeemed for fiat. As long as the consortium

maintains sufficient Bitcoin in Ledger 2, the system remains solvent at

any spot price above zero. An important nuance: spot price declines only

create reserve pressure when tokens are actually redeemed for fiat.

Tokens held long-term \-\-- whose holders chose BTCC precisely

because it is stable \-\-- do not present for redemption during
downturns.

Their purchasing power in BTCC terms is unaffected regardless of what

Bitcoin\\\'s spot price does.

**The ₿USD Is Not Cash**

A ₿USD token provides meaningfully better privacy properties than a

CBDC, but it is not equivalent to physical cash. Minting and redemption

require identity verification. Sidechain transfers are pseudonymous, not

anonymous \-\-- wallet addresses are visible on-chain, even if amounts
can

be obscured using confidential transaction features. Someone with

sufficient chain analysis capability and corroborating data could

potentially link transactions to individuals. Physical cash leaves no

record at all. Users who require complete transactional anonymity cannot

achieve it with a ₿USD, or with any blockchain-based instrument.

**Regulatory Risk**

Stablecoin regulation is evolving globally. The distributed consortium

structure provides some resilience \-\-- members operating in different

jurisdictions ensure no single regulatory action shuts down the entire

system. However, coordinated international regulation targeting

cryptocurrency stablecoins could threaten the token layer. The BTCC

denomination itself requires no regulatory permission to compute or use;

even if the token were banned, the unit of account persists as an open

standard that any party can calculate and reference.

**Consortium Coordination Risk**

Multiple independent companies must cooperate on reserve management, fee

structures, and operational standards. The critical design principle is

that the BTCADP specification is independent of the consortium\\\'s

governance. Even a complete governance failure leaves the denomination

intact. The unit of account outlasts the institution.

**Adoption Dependency**

The value proposition depends on merchants and users adopting BTCC as a

unit of account. Without critical mass, the system is a Bitcoin-backed

stablecoin with limited circulation. The properties are genuine, but

they must be demonstrated in practice before they drive broad adoption.

This is a go-to-market challenge rather than a structural flaw.

**VIII. Historical Timing**

Two conditions had to develop independently before the ₿USD model became

feasible. The first is the BTCC denomination\\\'s stability. The

mathematical property that makes BTCC useful for commerce \-\-- its

insensitivity to any single day\\\'s price movement \-\-- is a function
of

how many days of price history the cumulative average incorporates. In

2011, with roughly 365 days of history, a single day\\\'s price could

shift the BTCC price by 0.3%. As of early 2026, with over 6,200 days,

the same sensitivity has fallen below 0.01%. There is no shortcut to

this accumulation. It is the product of time.

The second condition is the existence of Bitcoin treasury companies as a

class of institutional participant. The first significant corporate

Bitcoin treasury allocation was announced in August 2020. The cohort of

publicly traded companies holding Bitcoin as a primary reserve asset at

sufficient scale to backstop a meaningful stablecoin is a product of

Bitcoin\\\'s maturation since then. The ₿USD architecture asks them not
to

take a new risk, but to monetize an existing one: to convert the

downside exposure inherent in their business model into a

revenue-generating service.

Both conditions have now been met. The timing is also relevant in a

second sense. CBDC development is accelerating globally. The window in

which a Bitcoin-backed alternative can be established before digital

sovereign currencies become the default infrastructure for retail

payments is not unlimited. The ₿USD architecture does not require

regulatory approval to begin operating. It requires coordination among a

small number of institutions that have already demonstrated their

commitment to Bitcoin as a reserve asset and their capacity to operate

at institutional scale.

**Conclusion**

The case for ₿USDs as a structural alternative to CBDCs rests on a

fundamental difference in what kind of money each instrument represents.

A CBDC is digitized sovereign money: faster to transfer, easier to

program, and equipped with control capabilities that physical cash does

not have. A ₿USD is something categorically different: a digital

currency backed by a scarce asset no issuer can produce, priced in

a unit determined by arithmetic rather than policy, and structured so

that the freedom properties of physical cash \-\-- privacy, censorship

resistance, protection from behavioral programming \-\-- are preserved
in

the digital medium.

The CBDC offers efficiency. It does not offer financial autonomy, and it

does not offer monetary integrity. In fact, the properties that make it

efficient \-\-- programmability, central record-keeping, administrative

control of balances \-\-- are precisely the properties that eliminate
the

financial autonomy that physical cash provides. And the property it

retains from conventional fiat money \-\-- the issuer\\\'s ability to
expand

the supply at will \-\-- guarantees the same purchasing power erosion
that

has been the defining feature of central bank money since its inception.

The digital format changes neither of these facts.

This is the inflation point stated plainly: every major central bank has

an explicit mandate to devalue its currency at 2% per year. This is not

a side effect. It is the policy goal. At that rate, a unit of currency

held over a 35-year working career loses half its real value. A CBDC

does not disrupt this mechanism. It accelerates it by removing physical

cash \-\-- the only instrument that has historically allowed holders to

partially exit the devaluation by holding a form of money the central

bank cannot directly program or penalize. A CBDC holder surrenders the

inflation protection of cash while gaining the surveillance exposure of

a digital account. That is not an upgrade.

The BTCC denomination moves in the opposite direction. Not because the

consortium has made a promise to defend its value. Not because a policy

committee has voted to maintain purchasing power. But because the

cumulative average of a scarce asset with a long-run upward price

trajectory rises over time as a matter of arithmetic. A ₿USD holder\'s

token maintains its \$1 peg \-\-- but the reserves backing that token

strengthen with every passing day, and the ₿C denomination that governs

the system\'s issuance ceiling appreciates steadily. This is the

deflationary property of the denomination, and it is as irreversible as

the inflation of fiat currencies, though it operates in the opposite

direction. A participant who moves from ₿USD into the ₿C-denominated

economy \-\-- or into a ₿ond savings product \-\-- captures that

appreciation directly.

  *\*A CBDC is a promise that your money will be worth less tomorrow
  than*

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  *it is today, enforced by the legal monopoly of a sovereign authority*

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  *and backed by the full faith of a government that prints its own*

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  *bills. The ₿C denomination that underpins ₿USD gets more valuable as time*

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  *passes, governed by arithmetic and secured by a reserve asset with a*

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  *fixed supply. The choice between them is not complicated.\**

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

The ₿USD does not solve every problem. It is not anonymous in the way

that physical cash is anonymous. Minting and redemption require identity

verification. Regulatory risk is real. Adoption requires effort. And the

model\\\'s viability depends on Bitcoin\\\'s long-term price trajectory
\-\--

the same thesis every Bitcoin treasury company has already committed to.

What the ₿USD offers that no CBDC can offer is this: a digital currency

whose issuer cannot expand its supply by decree, cannot surveil routine

transactions, cannot freeze balances held in self-custody, cannot apply

spending restrictions through software, and cannot be used as an

instrument of behavioral control by sovereign authorities. And a

denomination that, as a structural consequence of its design, returns

purchasing power to its holders over time rather than extracting it from

them. These properties are not the result of good policy choices that

could be reversed. They are structural consequences of using a finite,

decentralized reserve asset and an arithmetic denomination. They cannot

be changed without changing the architecture itself.

Bitcoin solved the problem of digital scarcity. The BTCC denomination

converts that scarcity into a stable, appreciating unit of account. The

treasury consortium provides the institutional infrastructure for

issuance and redemption. And the architecture as a whole produces a form

of digital money that is, for the first time, capable of rivaling

central bank currency issuance \-\-- not by replicating the central bank

model with different managers, but by replacing its foundational

assumption: that stable money requires a trusted central authority with

discretionary power over the supply.

It does not. The mathematics can be trusted instead.
