# BTCADP Content Strategy & Audience Map ## Strategic Framework **Objective:** Get the BTCADP framework implemented by Bitcoin treasury companies. **Method:** Build a pressure funnel where each audience tier creates social proof, credibility, and conversation that converges on the decision makers. **Source Material:** *The Bitcoin Bridge* (BBB) — 30 chapters, 9 parts, 5 appendices. **Existing Breakout Articles (pulled from site):** 9 articles covering portions of BBB. --- ## The Funnel ``` TIER 1: Content Creators & Influencers ──── Visibility TIER 2: Financial Media & Journalists ───── Amplification TIER 3: Analysts & Fund Managers ────────── Credibility TIER 4: Independent Researchers ─────────── Rigor TIER 5: Academics ──────────────────────── Legitimacy & Citability TIER 6: Developers & Technical Builders ─── Feasibility TIER 7: Regulators & Policy Makers ──────── Regulatory Clarity TIER 8: Treasury Company Shareholders ───── Bottom-Up Pressure TIER 9: Treasury Company Decision Makers ── IMPLEMENTATION ``` Each tier creates conditions that make the next tier's engagement more likely, and all roads lead to Tier 9. --- ## Missing Target Audiences The original four (content creators, analysts, researchers, academics) are strong but incomplete. Here are the audiences you missed and why they matter: ### Tier 2 — Financial Media & Journalists **Why they matter:** They're the amplification layer between content creators and institutional decision makers. A Forbes article, a Bloomberg opinion piece, or a CoinDesk deep-dive reaches treasury company boards in ways a Bitcoin Twitter thread does not. Journalists need data points, a clear narrative, and a "why now" hook. They are the bridge between online conversation and boardroom awareness. ### Tier 6 — Bitcoin Developers & Technical Builders **Why they matter:** Someone has to build this. Liquid developers, Lightning developers, wallet builders, sidechain engineers — these are the people who would implement ₿USD tokens, build the consortium infrastructure, and create the wallet experience. If they're not excited about the architecture, it doesn't get built regardless of what the treasury companies decide. They need specs, not narratives. ### Tier 7 — Regulators & Policy Makers **Why they matter:** Stablecoin legislation is being written *right now*. The GENIUS Act and similar frameworks are defining what stablecoins can and cannot do. If TBDC is not part of that conversation, the regulatory framework may be built in a way that excludes Bitcoin-backed stablecoins entirely. Policy makers need to understand TBDC as a market-based alternative that achieves their stated goals (financial inclusion, dollar stability, reduced systemic risk) without the surveillance architecture of CBDCs. This audience doesn't need to be convinced Bitcoin is good — they need to see that TBDC solves *their* problems. ### Tier 8 — Treasury Company Shareholders & Activist Investors **Why they matter:** Decision makers at Strategy, MARA, and Metaplanet don't operate in a vacuum. They answer to shareholders. If shareholders start asking "why aren't you exploring ₿USD?" at earnings calls, or if activist investors publish letters arguing that the current treasury model is one-dimensional, that creates pressure from below that complements the external credibility pressure from above. This is the pincer movement. Shareholders don't need the full technical architecture — they need the business case framed as "your company is leaving money on the table and ceding the monetary infrastructure to Tether." ### Also Worth Noting (Secondary Audiences) - **Merchants & Commerce Operators:** The demand side. Payment processors, e-commerce platforms, POS companies. They validate the "people would actually use this" claim. - **Emerging Market Fintech Builders:** The builders in Latin America, Africa, and Southeast Asia who are currently building on USDT/USDC and could build on ₿USD instead. - **Bitcoin Miners:** Aligned incentives — more BTC demand = higher prices = more mining revenue. They're potential consortium advocates. --- ## Content Inventory: What Exists ### 9 Pulled Articles (currently offline) | # | File | Title | Word Count | Core Topic | Community* *Goal: Get people talking. Make the ideas visible.* #### Paper 1A: "Stablecoins Are Stealing Bitcoin's Future — And It's a Design Problem" - **Format:** 1,500–2,000 words. Punchy. Provocative headline. - **Hook:** ARK's $300K price target reduction. Every dollar in USDT is a dollar that didn't buy Bitcoin. - **Argument:** This isn't inevitable. It's a design choice. Change the reserve asset, change the outcome. - **BBB Sources:** Ch. 1 (Onboarding Problem), Ch. 4 (Stablecoin Compromise), Ch. 7 (Unfinished Business) - **Existing Material:** `report-bitcoin-stablecoin.html` (Executive Summary + Section 1), `pragmatic-hyperbitcoinization.html` (opening sections) - **What's New:** Needs to be tighter, more provocative, less technical. Written for sharing, not studying. - **CTA:** "There's a framework that solves this. Here's how it works → [link to Paper 1B]" #### Paper 1B: "Three Worlds: Why You Need ₿C (Not Just BTC)" - **Format:** Visual explainer, 1,500 words + graphics - **Hook:** One paycheck, three strategies, drastically different outcomes over 10 years. - **Argument:** The fiat treadmill destroys purchasing power. The BTC round trip is tax-inefficient and volatile. ₿C is the bridge that lets you exit fiat once. - **BBB Sources:** Ch. 23 (Three Worlds), Ch. 10 (The Denomination) - **Existing Material:** `three-worlds.html` — this is already well-structured for this audience - **What's New:** Minor reframing. Emphasize the personal/relatable angle. This is the piece a podcaster can walk through in 10 minutes. - **CTA:** Links to Paper 1C and the full BBB #### Paper 1C: "Money That Protects You vs. Money That Reports on You" - **Format:** 1,800–2,200 words. Freedom/sovereignty angle. - **Hook:** CBDCs are being built in 130+ countries. They program money to control holders. There's an alternative that programs money to *defend* holders. - **Argument:** TBDC is the structural inverse of CBDC. Same technology, opposite philosophy. - **BBB Sources:** Ch. 5 (Surveillance Currency), Ch. 16 (TBDC vs. CBDC), Ch. 17 (Defensive Programmability) - **Existing Material:** `defensive-programmability.html`, `article-tbdc-vs-cbdc.html` - **What's New:** Combine and simplify the two existing articles. Lead with the threat, then introduce the solution. Less technical detail on the defensive toolkit, more emphasis on the philosophical inversion. - **CTA:** "The technical defense mechanisms → [link to full defensive programmability paper for devs]" #### Paper 1D: "A Letter to Maximalists" - **Format:** 1,200–1,500 words. Direct, respectful, Bitcoin-native voice. - **Hook:** "This framework doesn't compromise Bitcoin. It uses Bitcoin's own price history to build what Bitcoin needs to win." - **Argument:** ₿C isn't an altcoin. It's a mathematical fact derived from Bitcoin. Everything in this system purchases Bitcoin. The bridge is designed to close itself. - **BBB Sources:** Ch. 28 (For the Maximalists), Ch. 29 (What Bitcoiners Can Do), Ch. 27 (Bridge Closes Itself) - **Existing Material:** Sections of `pragmatic-hyperbitcoinization.html` - **What's New:** This doesn't exist as a standalone piece. Needs to be written fresh. The tone matters enormously — it has to speak Bitcoin-native language without being defensive. - **CTA:** "Read the full framework → [BBB link]. Verify everything yourself." --- ### PHASE 2 — Build Credibility (Weeks 3–8) *Target: Analysts, Fund Managers, Financial Media* *Goal: Make the economics legible to people who evaluate financial products.* #### Paper 2A: "₿C as a Pricing Denomination: The Math Behind the Stability" - **Format:** 2,500–3,500 words. Data-driven. Charts and tables. - **Hook:** A denomination that moves 0.04% per day, appreciates 19–125% annually, and becomes more stable every day — derived entirely from Bitcoin's own price history. - **Argument:** Walk through the cumulative average mechanics, the daily drift tolerances, the merchant margin effect, and the comparison to fiat inflation. - **BBB Sources:** Ch. 9 (BTCADP), Ch. 10 (₿C), Appendix B (BTCADP Spec) - **Existing Material:** `btcc-pricing-denomination.html` — good foundation but needs data visualization - **What's New:** Add historical charts from the CSV data. Show ₿C's actual trajectory. Show the daily drift distribution. Show the comparison to CPI. This is where the data files become critical. Analysts want to see the numbers, not just the argument. - **Data Required:** `btcadp_historical.csv`, `btcadp_btcc_historical.csv` #### Paper 2B: "The ₿USD Reserve Architecture: How Bitcoin Backs a Dollar Peg" - **Format:** 3,000–4,000 words. Structured like an analyst note. - **Hook:** A stablecoin backed by the scarcest asset in existence, with a reserve architecture that gets stronger as it scales — the inverse of traditional banking. - **Argument:** Four-ledger system, coverage ratio mechanics, the virtuous cycle, why scale reduces risk instead of increasing it. - **BBB Sources:** Ch. 11 (₿USD), Ch. 13 (Reserve Architecture), Ch. 18 (Risks) - **Existing Material:** `case-study-currency-layer.html`, `case-study-hard-money.html` - **What's New:** Merge the two case studies into a single analyst-grade paper. Add scenario analysis (what happens at BTC -50%, -70%, -90%). Add the coverage ratio math. Frame it as "here's how you'd model this." - **CTA:** "Full technical specification → [₿USD White Paper]" #### Paper 2C: "The $317 Billion Opportunity: Redirecting Stablecoin Capital into Bitcoin" - **Format:** 2,000–3,000 words. Macro/thematic piece. - **Hook:** The stablecoin market grew $100B in 2025. Every dollar went into Treasuries. What if it went into Bitcoin instead? - **Argument:** Model the demand impact. Reference the ARK analysis. Show what even 5%, 10%, 20% capture of stablecoin flows means for BTC price and treasury company valuations. - **BBB Sources:** Ch. 4 (Stablecoin Compromise), Ch. 15 (Economics for Each Party), Ch. 25 (Virtuous Cycle) - **Existing Material:** `report-bitcoin-stablecoin.html` (Sections 8, 10, 11) - **What's New:** Needs to be reframed as a market opportunity piece, not a framework overview. Analysts want "how big is this" and "what's the timeline." #### Paper 2D (Media-Specific): "Why Bitcoin's Next Bull Case Isn't Price — It's Infrastructure" - **Format:** 1,500–2,000 words. Op-ed / feature pitch format. - **Hook:** Everyone's modeling Bitcoin's price. Almost no one is modeling Bitcoin's monetary infrastructure. That's the bigger story. - **Argument:** Treasury companies are sitting on $50B+ in Bitcoin with no monetary utility layer. The framework exists. The question is whether they'll build it or watch Tether become the monetary infrastructure of the developing world. - **BBB Sources:** Ch. 19 (If Not Treasury Companies), Ch. 6 (Two Futures), Ch. 20 (Case for Building) - **Existing Material:** `innovate-or-die_v1.html`, `case-for-building.html` - **What's New:** Needs to be pitched as journalism, not advocacy. Feature-story structure. Quotes and data points a journalist can use. --- ### PHASE 3 — Establish Rigor (Weeks 6–16) *Target: Researchers, Academics* *Goal: Make the framework citable and extensible.* #### Paper 3A: "The Two-Sphere Monetary Model: A Framework for Parallel Currency Systems" - **Format:** 4,000–6,000 words. Academic structure (abstract, lit review, framework, analysis, conclusion). - **Hook:** The fiat economy and the Bitcoin economy are parallel monetary systems operating without a common unit of account. This paper proposes a framework for understanding their interaction and the role of a derived denomination as a translation layer. - **Argument:** Formalize the two-sphere model. Define the properties a bridge denomination must have. Show that ₿C satisfies those properties mathematically. - **BBB Sources:** Ch. 22 (Two Spheres), Ch. 8 (Functions of Money), Ch. 10 (₿C) - **Existing Material:** Sections of `pragmatic-hyperbitcoinization.html`, BBB Ch. 22 - **What's New:** This is substantially new writing. The BBB makes the argument narratively; this needs to make it formally. Literature review connecting to existing monetary theory (Hayek, Selgin, etc.). #### Paper 3B: "BTCADP and ₿C: Mathematical Properties of a Cumulative Average Denomination" - **Format:** 3,000–5,000 words. Technical/quantitative. - **Hook:** A formal analysis of the statistical properties of a unit of account derived from the cumulative arithmetic mean of daily asset prices. - **Argument:** Convergence behavior, sensitivity analysis (how much does day N+1 move the average as N grows), volatility compression, comparison to other averaging methods (EMA, SMA), the "ratchet" effect (why the average rises structurally). - **BBB Sources:** Ch. 9 (BTCADP), Appendix B (Specification) - **Existing Material:** BTCADP specification, historical data CSVs - **What's New:** Mostly new. This is the quantitative paper that gives academics something to cite and extend. It needs formal proofs or at least formal demonstrations of the convergence properties. - **Data Required:** Both CSV files for empirical analysis #### Paper 3C: "Open Research Questions in Bitcoin-Denominated Financial Instruments" - **Format:** 2,000–3,000 words. Research agenda paper. - **Hook:** "We don't claim to have answered every question. Here are the ones that need answering." - **Argument:** Explicitly lay out the open problems that would benefit from academic research. This turns academics into participants rather than observers. - **Open Questions to Include:** - Actuarial modeling of ₿OND maturity distributions under various volatility regimes - Game-theoretic analysis of consortium dynamics (when does defection become rational?) - Optimal reserve ratio sizing — what does the literature on bank capital requirements suggest? - ₿C as a deflation-adjusted unit of account — connections to the deflation literature (Booth, Selgin) - Empirical analysis of ₿C's purchasing power stability vs. CPI, gold, SDR - Agent-based modeling of the virtuous cycle — does it hold under adversarial conditions? - Legal classification of TBDC across jurisdictions - Privacy properties of the defensive programmability framework - **BBB Sources:** Ch. 18 (Risks), Ch. 21 (Objections), throughout - **Existing Material:** Scattered across BBB - **What's New:** Entirely new. This is a strategic document designed to recruit academic labor. --- ### PHASE 4 — Technical Feasibility (Weeks 8–16) *Target: Developers, Sidechain Engineers, Wallet Builders* *Goal: Prove it can be built. Attract contributors.* #### Paper 4A: "₿USD Implementation Guide: From Specification to Sidechain" - **Format:** 3,000–5,000 words. Technical documentation style. - **Hook:** Here's how you'd actually build this on Liquid (or another sidechain). - **Argument:** Walk through the token lifecycle (minting, circulation, redemption) at the implementation level. Address the specific technical decisions: which sidechain, how the BTCADP oracle works, how the four-ledger system maps to actual Bitcoin addresses, how defensive programmability is implemented at the token level. - **BBB Sources:** Ch. 11 (₿USD), Ch. 13 (Reserve Architecture), Ch. 17 (Defensive Programmability), Appendix D (₿USD White Paper) - **Existing Material:** White papers provide the specification; this translates it into implementation guidance - **What's New:** Substantially new. The white papers describe *what*; this describes *how*. Needs input from someone with Liquid/sidechain development experience. #### Paper 4B: "Defensive Programmability: Technical Specification" - **Format:** 3,000–4,000 words. Spec-level detail. - **Hook:** The six defensive mechanisms, specified precisely enough that a developer could implement them. - **Argument:** For each mechanism (BTC-default redemption, time-weighted fees, volume-triggered escalation, velocity limits, ₿C lateral conversion, notice periods), provide: trigger conditions, parameters, pseudocode, edge cases. - **BBB Sources:** Ch. 17 (Defensive Programmability) - **Existing Material:** `defensive-programmability.html` — strong conceptual foundation - **What's New:** The existing article explains the philosophy and the mechanisms. This paper needs to specify them precisely enough for implementation. --- ### PHASE 5 — Policy & Regulatory (Weeks 8–20) *Target: Regulators, Policy Makers, Think Tanks* *Goal: Get TBDC into the policy conversation before stablecoin regulation solidifies.* #### Paper 5A: "Treasury-Backed Digital Currency: A Market Alternative to Central Bank Digital Currency" - **Format:** 3,000–5,000 words. Policy paper format. - **Hook:** 130+ countries are exploring CBDCs. The policy conversation assumes the only alternative is fiat-backed stablecoins. There is a third option. - **Argument:** TBDC achieves the stated policy goals of CBDCs (financial inclusion, payment efficiency, reduced cash handling costs) without the surveillance architecture. It is issued by regulated, publicly traded entities. It is fully collateralized and auditable. They don't care about Bitcoin ideology. They care about financial stability, consumer protection, privacy law, and systemic risk. The language needs to be completely different from the Bitcoin-native versions. --- ### PHASE 6 — The Pincer (Weeks 12–24) *Target: Treasury Company Decision Makers + Their Shareholders* *Goal: Create the conditions where "no" is harder to justify than "yes."* #### Paper 6A: "Innovate or Cede the Future: A Strategic Brief for Bitcoin Treasury Companies" - **Format:** 3,000–4,000 words. It adds fee income, deepens reserves through the virtuous cycle, and creates a competitive moat that pure buy-and-hold cannot replicate.
