₿USD — A Bitcoin Currency Layer

How Treasury-Backed Digital Currency Provides a Bitcoin-Native Foundation for Everyday Commerce

A Framework for Bitcoin Treasury Companies  •  btcadp.org  •  March 2026  •  CC0 Public Domain

This paper examines how a consortium of Bitcoin treasury companies can issue Treasury-Backed Digital Currency (₿USD) — a USD-pegged stablecoin backed by Bitcoin reserves — to provide a currency layer for everyday commerce. The core argument is that ₿USD solves Bitcoin's volatility problem for commerce without abandoning Bitcoin, using Bitcoin Currency (₿C) as the mechanism that makes the collateral floor credible.

The paper is addressed to Bitcoin treasury companies evaluating participation in a ₿USD consortium. It describes the architecture, the economic model, and the structural dynamics that make this product viable at scale.

The central problem ₿USD solves: Bitcoin's spot price volatility makes it impractical for everyday commerce. Existing stablecoins solve this by abandoning Bitcoin entirely — holding dollars, US Treasuries, or bank deposits. ₿USD solves it differently: one token equals one dollar, backed entirely by Bitcoin held on the base layer, with the ₿C price serving as the conservative collateral floor that makes the peg credible through Bitcoin's cyclical volatility.

1. The Problem

Bitcoin has operated continuously since January 2009. Lightning provides throughput. Settlement is secure and final. What has not yet been built on top of Bitcoin is a stable denomination layer for everyday commerce — Bitcoin's spot price can move 10% in a day, making it impractical for the pricing, invoicing, and budgeting that constitute most economic activity.

The stablecoin market has grown to over $200 billion in response to this problem. USDT and USDC have achieved genuine adoption for payments and commerce. But every dollar that flows into a fiat-backed stablecoin goes into US Treasuries or bank accounts — not into Bitcoin. Fiat stablecoins solve the volatility problem for users while actively competing with Bitcoin for monetary adoption.

For Bitcoin treasury companies, this represents a structural opportunity. The same demand that has built a $200 billion fiat stablecoin market exists for a Bitcoin-backed alternative. Capturing even a fraction of it would create direct, sustained Bitcoin demand — while generating fee revenue on a growing float.

2. The Architecture

₿USD is built on three components. Each is independent. Each depends only on the component below it.

Foundation
BTCADP

The reference price. A daily Bitcoin price computed from qualifying exchanges using a 25% trimmed mean. Any party can reproduce it independently. The methodology is the authority — no institution required.

Denomination
Bitcoin Currency (₿C)

The collateral floor. The cumulative arithmetic mean of every historical BTCADP since genesis. Moves approximately 0.04% per day. No issuer. No reserves. Pure mathematics. Used to cap ₿USD issuance conservatively.

Instrument
₿USD

The monetary instrument. One token equals one US dollar. Issued by the consortium. Backed by Bitcoin in a four-ledger reserve system (two per product). Redeemable for fiat at any time.

A disruption to any component does not propagate downward. If every ₿USD issuer ceased operations, the ₿C denomination would still be computable. If ₿C were abandoned, BTCADP would remain a valid reference price. Each component can be evaluated independently.

3. How ₿C Makes the Peg Credible

The ₿C price is the conservative anchor of the entire system. Total ₿USD issuance is capped at the ₿C-denominated value of the consortium's combined Bitcoin holdings — not spot value. Because ₿C currently sits at approximately one-quarter of spot price, every Bitcoin in the consortium's treasury supports only $18,700 of ₿USD issuance rather than the full $70,000 spot value. The system is structurally overcollateralized at all times by approximately 3.75x.

For the peg to fail, Bitcoin's spot price would need to fall below the ₿C price and remain there permanently. The ₿C price is Bitcoin's own lifetime cumulative average — a level that spot has never sustained below in Bitcoin's history. The floor is not a promise made by the consortium. It is a mathematical property of Bitcoin's price history.

As Bitcoin's spot price rises over time, the ₿C price rises with it — slowly and permanently, as each new day's price is absorbed into the cumulative average. The floor rises. Issuance capacity expands. The system becomes more robust as it succeeds.

4. The Two-Ledger Reserve System

Each consortium member maintains two Bitcoin ledgers on the base layer. Both hold actual Bitcoin in publicly verifiable wallets. Reserves are not lent, staked, or rehypothecated.

1️⃣
Ledger 1 — Issuance Pool
Bitcoin purchased with incoming consumer USD at the moment of minting. Directly backs outstanding tokens. Appreciates at BTC spot rate. Generates surplus as spot rises above issuance price.
Primary backing
2️⃣
Ledger 2 — Reserve Backstop
Bitcoin drawn from each consortium member's existing treasury holdings. Covers redemptions when Ledger 1 is insufficient. Size of Ledger 2 determines the system's bear market depth.
Backstop

The reserve system only matters at the boundaries — when fiat enters (minting) and when fiat exits (redemption). Tokens circulating between wallets as a medium of exchange create no reserve pressure at any BTC spot price. A million ₿USD transactions can occur without a single satoshi moving on-chain.

A user who mints, spends, receives, and saves in ₿USD for months has interacted with the Bitcoin base layer exactly twice — at entry and at exit. Everything in between is token transfers denominated in dollars, with no exposure to BTC spot price.

6. The Currency Layer Effect

As ₿USD adoption deepens, the mental model for users shifts. Early adopters think of ₿USD as "digital dollars" — pegged to fiat, redeemable on demand. As more commerce occurs natively in ₿USD — salaries, rent, invoicing, savings — the need to redeem for fiat diminishes. Tokens stay on the currency layer.

When a merchant prices goods in ₿USD, a worker receives a salary in ₿USD, and a lender denominates a loan in ₿USD, redemption pressure approaches zero. Ledger 1 and Ledger 2 accumulate Bitcoin appreciation without outflow. The coverage ratio compounds continuously. The consortium's collateral position strengthens without any action required.

The reserve requirement is the bridge. It is what makes ₿USD credible long enough for the currency layer to reach adoption. Once enough commerce occurs natively in ₿USD, the bridge is no longer the critical path — but it remains in place, getting stronger, as collateral accumulates.

7. The Virtuous Cycle

Every ₿USD token minted requires the consortium to purchase Bitcoin. Consumer demand for ₿USD is structurally identical to Bitcoin demand. As adoption grows, four things happen simultaneously.

01
Minting creates Bitcoin demand

Every ₿USD minted requires purchasing Bitcoin at spot. Commerce-driven demand is persistent — it does not reverse when sentiment shifts.

02
Rising spot expands issuance capacity

As BTC spot rises, the ₿C-denominated value of consortium holdings increases. The issuance ceiling rises. More ₿USD can be issued. More BTC gets purchased.

03
Fee reinvestment deepens reserves

100% of fee revenue is deployed to purchase additional Bitcoin until coverage thresholds are met. The threshold is determined by the coverage ratio formula, not by board discretion.

04
Scale reduces redemption risk

As more commerce occurs natively in ₿USD, the proportion of tokens redeemed for fiat declines. The system becomes structurally safer as it grows — the inverse of traditional fractional reserve banking.

8. The Economic Case for Treasury Companies

A treasury company that simply holds Bitcoin earns a return equal to spot price appreciation. The ₿USD model produces returns that exceed holding alone, through three distinct revenue streams.

Fee revenue. A holding fee of 1–2% annually on the outstanding ₿USD float generates continuous income regardless of Bitcoin's price. On $10 billion in circulation, that is $100–200 million annually.

Collateral appreciation. The Bitcoin in Ledger 1 appreciates at the spot rate. The ₿USD liability it backs is fixed at $1.00. As spot rises, the surplus between asset value and liability widens. At $140,000 BTC spot, a token issued at $70,000 has a Ledger 1 value of $2.00 against a $1.00 obligation — 100% surplus captured on redemption or held as reserve depth.

Bitcoin accumulation. Fee revenue reinvested into Bitcoin during the bootstrapping phase compounds the collateral base. The consortium is not merely issuing a product — it is systematically accumulating the reserve asset that makes the product credible, funded by users who are voluntarily paying for a better monetary instrument.

9. Conclusion

₿USD provides what the stablecoin market has not yet delivered: a USD-pegged digital currency backed entirely by Bitcoin, with a collateral floor that is mathematically determined rather than institutionally promised. The ₿C price — Bitcoin's own cumulative price history — is the mechanism that makes overcollateralization credible and automatic.

For Bitcoin treasury companies, ₿USD is not a departure from their existing mandate. It is an extension of it. They already hold the collateral. They already believe in Bitcoin's long-run appreciation. ₿USD converts that conviction into a product that creates sustained Bitcoin demand, generates fee revenue on a growing float, and deepens their reserve position through every cycle — funded by the very users who benefit from the product.

Every user who enters the ₿USD economy is a Bitcoin user. Every transaction settles on Bitcoin infrastructure. Every new token requires Bitcoin to be acquired. The currency layer does not compete with Bitcoin. It fulfills Bitcoin's original purpose — peer-to-peer electronic cash — by providing the denomination layer the protocol was never designed to include, and never needed to.
