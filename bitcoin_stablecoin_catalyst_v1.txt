**1. The Stablecoin Problem Is Real**

In November 2025, Cathie Wood and ARK Invest reduced their bull-case

2030 Bitcoin price target from \\\$1.5 million to \\\$1.2 million. The

reason was specific and data-driven: fiat-denominated stablecoins are

absorbing the emerging-market demand that ARK\'s model had previously

assigned to Bitcoin. In Wood\'s words, stablecoins are \"usurping part
of

the role we thought Bitcoin would play.\"

ARK\'s January 2026 Big Ideas report formalized the revision. The firm

sharply reduced its expectations for Bitcoin\'s role as an

emerging-market safe haven, citing rapid stablecoin adoption in

developing economies. The digital gold total addressable market was

increased by 37% (reflecting gold\'s 64.5% market cap expansion in
2025),

but the emerging-market medium-of-exchange component was cut

significantly. The net effect was a base-case 2030 target of

approximately \\\$762,000 per BTC, implying a market capitalization of

roughly \\\$16 trillion.

The data supports the concern. As of early 2026, the total stablecoin

market capitalization exceeds \\\$317 billion. It grew by approximately

\\\$100 billion in 2025 alone, following \\\$70 billion of growth in
2024.

Tether (USDT) controls roughly 60% of the market at \\\$187 billion in

supply, with Circle\'s USDC holding approximately 25% at \\\$76 billion.

Monthly stablecoin transaction volumes approached \\\$970 billion by

August 2025, with projections pointing toward \\\$1 trillion in monthly

flows by late 2026. In Latin America, 71% of stablecoin activity is tied

to cross-border payments. In sub-Saharan Africa and Southeast Asia,

stablecoins are functioning as dollar-access infrastructure for

populations with limited banking services.

These are not speculative figures. The stablecoin market is growing

faster than Bitcoin adoption in the use cases ARK originally modeled.

The people ARK expected to adopt Bitcoin as a store of value and payment

mechanism in emerging markets are instead adopting dollar-pegged

stablecoins. The revision to the price target is a rational response to

observable data.

**2. The Hidden Assumption**

ARK\'s model treats the stablecoin market as structurally competitive

with Bitcoin. This is correct given the current design of fiat

stablecoins, but it rests on an assumption that is worth making

explicit: it assumes that all stablecoins will be fiat-backed.

Consider what happens when someone purchases a USDT or USDC token. They

send dollars to an issuer. The issuer holds those dollars (or

dollar-equivalent instruments, primarily US Treasury bills) as reserves.

The capital flow is: user → stablecoin issuer → US government debt. At

\\\$317 billion in aggregate supply, stablecoin issuers are now among
the

largest purchasers of US Treasuries globally. Tether alone held over

\\\$100 billion in Treasury bills as of late 2025.

From the perspective of Bitcoin\'s addressable market, every dollar that

enters a fiat stablecoin is a dollar that does not enter Bitcoin. The

demand is captured by the traditional financial system\'s debt

instruments. The blockchain provides the settlement rail, but the

economic substance is a digital dollar deposit backed by sovereign debt.

Bitcoin is excluded from the transaction entirely.

This is why ARK\'s model correctly identifies stablecoins as competitive

with Bitcoin. The current generation of stablecoins is, in economic

terms, a distribution mechanism for US government debt on blockchain

rails. The technology is Bitcoin-adjacent. The capital flow is

Bitcoin-absent.

The question for a long-term model is whether this is an inherent

property of stablecoins as a category, or whether it is a design choice

specific to the first generation. If a stablecoin could be constructed

with Bitcoin as the reserve asset instead of Treasuries, the demand

vector would invert: every token minted would require Bitcoin

acquisition at spot rather than Treasury purchase. The stablecoin market

would become additive to Bitcoin demand rather than competitive with it.

**3. A Theoretical Bitcoin-Backed Stablecoin**

A theoretical framework for such an instrument has been proposed under

the name Treasury-Backed Digital Currency (₿USD). The model is not yet

operational. No tokens have been issued. What exists is a published

specification, a defined denomination, and a structural argument for how

the components could be assembled. The following description is of a

theoretical system, not a deployed product.

The core problem any Bitcoin-backed stablecoin must solve is

denomination. Bitcoin\'s spot price volatility\-\--routinely 5\--10% in
a

single week\-\--makes it unsuitable as a unit of account for commerce or

savings. Any stablecoin denominated directly in BTC inherits this

volatility and fails the basic stability requirement.

**The BTCC Denomination**

The ₿USD framework proposes a denomination called BTCC (Bitcoin

Currency), defined as the arithmetic mean of every historical Bitcoin

Average Daily Price (BTCADP) from the genesis block on January 3, 2009

through the previous completed day. The BTCADP itself is an open-source

reference price computed daily from trade data across qualifying

exchanges, using a published specification that any party can

independently reproduce.

The stability claim rests on the mathematical properties of cumulative

averages. As of early 2026, the BTCC denomination incorporates over

6,200 daily price observations. The sensitivity of the cumulative

average to any single day\'s price is approximately 1/6,200 of the

difference between that day\'s price and the current average. In
concrete

terms: a 10% single-day move in BTC spot would shift the BTCC price by

less than 0.01%. A 40% single-day crash would shift it by approximately

0.04%. These are arithmetic properties of cumulative averages, not

claims about market behavior.

The BTCC fiat-equivalent price as of early 2026 sits at approximately

\\\$18,000\-\--the cumulative average of all daily Bitcoin prices over
16

years of market history. Because Bitcoin\'s current spot price

substantially exceeds the historical average, the BTCC price appreciates

slowly over time as each new above-average observation pulls the

cumulative mean upward. This rate of appreciation decelerates as the

denominator grows.

**The Consortium Model**

The proposed issuance mechanism relies on a consortium of Bitcoin

treasury companies\-\--publicly traded firms whose primary business is

acquiring and holding Bitcoin as a reserve asset. In the ₿USD framework,

a customer sends fiat to a consortium member to purchase a ₿USD token.

The treasury company uses the incoming fiat to buy Bitcoin at spot and

deposits it into a transparent on-chain reserve. One ₿USD token,

pegged to one US dollar, is issued.

Two reserve ledgers are proposed. Ledger 1 (the issuance pool) holds

Bitcoin acquired with incoming fiat at the time of token minting. Ledger

2 (the reserve backstop) holds additional Bitcoin drawn from the

treasury company\'s existing holdings, which would guarantee redemptions

during periods when BTC spot has declined below the price at issuance.

Both ledgers would be held in publicly addressable Bitcoin wallets,

verifiable by any observer in real time.

The economic trade-off is straightforward. The customer trades
Bitcoin\'s

spot volatility for the BTCC denomination\'s stability. The treasury

company accepts the downside risk of backstopping redemptions during

spot declines, and in exchange captures the spread between Bitcoin\'s

spot appreciation rate and the BTCC denomination\'s much slower rate of

change. This spread widens as Bitcoin\'s spot price rises\-\--the core

thesis on which every Bitcoin treasury company has already staked its

balance sheet.

Tokens would circulate as issued assets on a Bitcoin sidechain. When one

user pays another, the token moves on the sidechain. No Bitcoin moves.

The reserves sit on Bitcoin\'s base layer, untouched by token

circulation. Bitcoin is bought at minting and sold only when a customer

exits the ecosystem by redeeming a token for fiat. As the ecosystem

matures and more commerce occurs natively in BTCC, the proportion of

tokens ever redeemed for fiat naturally declines\-\--a user who can
earn,

spend, and hold a denomination has diminishing reason to exit.

Everything between minting and the increasingly infrequent event of fiat

exit is token circulation.

**4. The Demand Inversion: The Core Analytical Insight**

This is the observation most relevant to a long-term Bitcoin price

model. The current stablecoin market routes capital away from Bitcoin. A

Bitcoin-backed stablecoin, if it achieved meaningful adoption, would

route capital toward it.

The mechanism is direct. Every fiat stablecoin minted creates demand for

US Treasuries. Every ₿USD token minted, under the proposed framework,

would create demand for Bitcoin at spot. The stablecoin layer\'s

relationship to Bitcoin changes from competitive to symbiotic. Rather

than absorbing demand that might otherwise flow to BTC, the stablecoin

layer would *generate* demand for BTC as a structural feature of its

operation.

To frame this quantitatively for modeling purposes, consider the current

stablecoin market at approximately \\\$320 billion. All of that capital

currently sits in Treasuries or dollar-equivalent instruments. None of

it is in Bitcoin. If a Bitcoin-backed stablecoin captured even a

fraction of this market, the capital would need to flow into BTC at

spot.

These numbers are worth contextualizing. At 10% capture of the current

stablecoin market, Bitcoin-backed stablecoin reserves would require

approximately 376,000 BTC\-\--roughly half of what Strategy alone

These are large figures, but the institutional infrastructure to support

The more consequential figure for a price model is not the static

snapshot but the flow. If the stablecoin market continues growing at its

current trajectory\-\--Standard Chartered has projected \\\$2 trillion

and

billion in Bitcoin holdings, requiring roughly 2.35 million BTC at

current prices. This is capital that, under the current architecture,

would flow entirely into US Treasuries.

It is worth stating clearly that these are theoretical calculations. No

Bitcoin-backed stablecoin at this scale has been tested. The adoption

rates are illustrative. The point is not to predict what will happen,

but to identify what the demand implications would be if the stablecoin

market\'s reserve composition changed.

**5. The Infrastructure Question**

A common objection to theoretical frameworks is that the required

infrastructure does not exist. In this case, the building blocks are

present, though they have not been assembled for this purpose.

As of March 2026, Strategy (formerly MicroStrategy) holds 738,731 BTC,

valued at over \\\$52 billion. MARA Holdings holds approximately 53,800

BTC. Twenty One Capital, backed by Tether, holds 43,514 BTC. Metaplanet,

the largest publicly traded Bitcoin treasury company in Asia, holds

35,102 BTC. In total, 193 publicly traded companies have adopted some

form of Bitcoin treasury strategy, with the top 10 alone holding well

over 1 million BTC collectively.

These companies hold Bitcoin that currently generates no direct yield.

The Bitcoin appreciates (or depreciates) in value, but it does not

produce cash flow while sitting in reserve. A Bitcoin-backed stablecoin

model would, in theory, allow treasury companies to generate fee revenue

from their holdings by pledging them as reserve backing for a

circulating stablecoin. The reserves are not lent, staked, or

rehypothecated\-\--but they are not permanently inert. Bitcoin is sold

from Ledger 1 when a customer exits the ₿USD ecosystem by redeeming a

token for fiat. If BTC spot has fallen below the minting price, the

treasury company must also sell Bitcoin from Ledger 2\-\--its own

pre-existing holdings\-\--to cover the shortfall. This is a real cost,
and

it is the treasury company\'s compensation risk for the spread it

captures during periods of appreciation. Crucially, tokens circulating

within the ecosystem\-\--moving between wallets as a medium of

exchange\-\--create no reserve pressure at any BTC spot price. As the

ecosystem matures and users can earn, spend, and transact in a stable

denomination, the proportion of tokens ever redeemed for fiat naturally

declines, reducing the frequency and magnitude of reserve sales over

time.

The reference price infrastructure also exists. The BTCADP specification

is published, open-source, and independently reproducible. It uses

per-exchange volume-weighted average prices aggregated via a trimmed

mean with equal exchange weighting and a sequence of algorithmic quality

filters. The methodology is the authority; no single institution is

required for the denomination to remain valid and computable.

What does not exist is the assembled product: no consortium has been

formed, no tokens have been issued, no sidechain deployment has

occurred, and no market test has been conducted. The gap between

available components and a functioning monetary instrument is

non-trivial. It requires coordination among multiple independent

companies, regulatory navigation, and a go-to-market effort against

entrenched incumbents with hundreds of billions in existing supply.

These are real barriers, not theoretical ones.

**6. Implications for Bitcoin Price Models**

ARK\'s current model treats the stablecoin market as a demand
competitor.

The \\\$300,000 reduction to the bull case reflects the model\'s
assumption

that stablecoin adoption in emerging markets directly subtracts from

Bitcoin\'s addressable market in those regions. This is the correct

treatment given the current composition of stablecoin reserves.

A Bitcoin-backed stablecoin, if it achieved meaningful scale, would

change that model input from negative to positive. Rather than

subtracting stablecoin market growth from Bitcoin\'s TAM, a portion of
it

would be added. The stablecoin market\'s growth rate\-\--currently among

the fastest in digital assets\-\--would become a Bitcoin demand driver

rather than a drag.

To be specific about what this means for the model: ARK\'s November 2025

revision subtracted approximately \\\$300,000 from the bull-case 2030

target. That figure corresponds to the emerging-market

medium-of-exchange TAM component that ARK reassigned from Bitcoin to

stablecoins. If a Bitcoin-backed stablecoin recaptured that
demand\-\--not

by replacing fiat stablecoins entirely, but by offering an alternative

that routes reserves into BTC\-\--the subtracted value would return to
the

model, and potentially more, because the stablecoin market has continued

growing since the revision was made.

A simple framework for incorporating this into a price model:

**Variable 1: Stablecoin market size at target date.** ARK\'s 2030

timeframe. Projections range from \\\$1\--2 trillion depending on the

source and assumptions about regulatory environment and adoption curves.

**Variable 2: Bitcoin-backed share.** The percentage of total stablecoin

supply backed by Bitcoin rather than Treasuries. Even conservative

assumptions (5\--10%) produce large absolute figures given the projected

market size.

**Variable 3: Demand impact.** The additional BTC that must be acquired

at spot to collateralize the Bitcoin-backed share. This is net new

demand that does not exist in the current model and would not exist

under a purely fiat-backed stablecoin regime.

At a projected \\\$1.5 trillion stablecoin market in 2030 and a 10%

Bitcoin-backed share, the reserve requirement would be \\\$150 billion
in

Bitcoin\-\--roughly 1.76 million BTC at current prices, or approximately

8.4% of the total 21 million supply. This is demand that ARK\'s current

model assigns a value of zero, because it assumes all stablecoins will

be fiat-backed.

It should be emphasized that this is a modeling exercise, not a

prediction. The Bitcoin-backed share could be zero if the model never

progresses beyond the theoretical stage. It could also exceed 10% if the

properties of the BTCC denomination\-\--slow appreciation rather than

guaranteed inflation, no issuer capable of supply expansion, transparent

on-chain reserves\-\--prove attractive to a meaningful segment of the

market. The point is that the current model treats the outcome as

structurally determined, when it may in fact be a variable worth

tracking.

**7. Risks and Limitations**

Any assessment of this framework should account for the following risks,

each of which could prevent the model from achieving meaningful scale.

**The framework is theoretical.** No ₿USD tokens have been issued. No

consortium has been formed. No market test has been conducted. The gap

between a published specification and a functioning monetary product is

substantial, and many sound theoretical frameworks never achieve

real-world adoption.

**Sustained Bitcoin price decline.** The reserve model depends on

consortium members\' willingness and ability to backstop redemptions

during periods when BTC spot falls below the price at which reserves

were acquired. A prolonged bear market would pressure reserve ratios and

could trigger a confidence crisis similar to those that have affected

algorithmic stablecoins. The two-ledger reserve design provides a

buffer, but it is not unlimited. This risk is real and should not be

understated.

\*\*The denomination\'s appreciation depends on Bitcoin\'s long-run
price

trajectory.\*\* The BTCC denomination appreciates as long as Bitcoin\'s

spot price remains above the historical cumulative average. If
Bitcoin\'s

price were to enter a multi-year structural decline below the cumulative

average, the denomination would depreciate. The stability property

(insensitivity to single-day moves) holds regardless, but the direction

of that slow drift would reverse.

**Regulatory uncertainty.** Stablecoin regulation is evolving. The

GENIUS Act in the US has provided some clarity for fiat-backed issuers,

but the regulatory treatment of a Bitcoin-backed stablecoin priced in

in a non-fiat unit of account is untested. Coordinated international

regulation targeting cryptocurrency stablecoins could constrain or

prevent the model\'s deployment.

**Entrenched competition.** USDT and USDC collectively control over 85%

of the stablecoin market. Network effects, exchange integrations, and

established liquidity make displacement difficult. A Bitcoin-backed

alternative would need to offer a compelling enough value proposition to

overcome the switching costs and inertia of a \\\$320 billion incumbent

market.

**Consortium coordination.** Multiple independent publicly traded

companies, each in its own regulatory jurisdiction, would need to agree

on reserve management, fee structures, operational standards, and

governance. Corporate coordination at this scale is difficult under the

best circumstances and is not guaranteed to succeed.

**Adoption dependency.** The demand inversion described in this article

only occurs if the Bitcoin-backed stablecoin achieves meaningful

adoption. A small pilot with limited circulation does not change the

model inputs for a long-term Bitcoin price target. The framework must

reach scale to matter, and scale requires merchant acceptance, user

adoption, and integration into existing payment infrastructure.

**8. Conclusion**

ARK Invest\'s concern about fiat stablecoins is well-founded. The
\\\$300

billion stablecoin market is absorbing emerging-market demand that

Bitcoin bulls had counted on, and the growth trajectory shows no sign of

slowing. The revision to the 2030 bull case is a rational response to

observed data.

What the current model does not incorporate is the possibility that the

stablecoin market\'s reserve composition could change. A Bitcoin-backed

stablecoin\-\--if one were to be built and achieve meaningful

scale\-\--would invert the demand relationship. Every token minted would

require Bitcoin acquisition at spot. The stablecoin market would become

a source of Bitcoin demand rather than a competitor for it.

The ₿USD framework described here proposes a specific mechanism for how

this could work: a consortium of existing Bitcoin treasury companies

issuing tokens priced in a cumulative-average-based unit of

account, backed by on-chain Bitcoin reserves. The components exist. The

specification is published. The institutional participants hold over 1

million BTC collectively. What has not been demonstrated is whether

these components can be assembled into a functioning product that

achieves adoption at scale. That remains an open question.

For the purposes of a long-term Bitcoin price model, the relevant

takeaway is this: the stablecoin market\'s impact on Bitcoin\'s

addressable market is not necessarily a fixed negative. It is a variable

whose sign depends on the reserve composition of the stablecoins that

capture the market. A model that treats it as permanently negative may

be underweighting a scenario worth tracking.
