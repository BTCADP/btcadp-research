The Case for a Bitcoin-Backed Stablecoin

How a Bitcoin-Native Monetary Ecosystem Could Be the Catalyst for Bitcoin Demand and Mainstream Adoption

Executive Summary

The stablecoin market has grown to $317 billion and is absorbing the emerging-market demand that Bitcoin bulls expected Bitcoin itself to capture. ARK Invest reduced its 2030 bull-case price target by $300,000 in direct response. The demand is real. The capital flows are real. The only question is where the reserve assets go: into US Treasury bills, or into Bitcoin.

A consortium of Bitcoin treasury companies has the institutional infrastructure, the reserve capital, and the Bitcoin thesis alignment to redirect that capital flow. The mechanism is a three-component ecosystem built on a single foundation: the BTCADP open reference price standard, a ₿ond savings product denominated in the ₿C unit of account, and ₿USD — a Treasury-Backed Digital Currency that maintains a hard $1 peg and functions as a medium of exchange.

1. The Problem: Bitcoin's Unfinished Business

Bitcoin has succeeded beyond most early projections as a store of value, a settlement network, and a treasury reserve asset. It has not yet succeeded as everyday money. The gap between what Bitcoin was designed to be and what it has become is not a technical failure. It is an interface failure — a presentation problem that has been solved in the wrong direction.

The wrong direction is the fiat stablecoin. When someone purchases USDT or USDC, they send dollars to a centralized issuer, the issuer holds those dollars in US Treasury bills, and a blockchain token is issued in return. The payment rail is digital. The reserve asset is sovereign debt. Bitcoin is absent from the transaction entirely. At $317 billion in aggregate supply — growing by approximately $100 billion per year — the stablecoin market has become one of the largest purchasers of US government debt on earth. Tether alone holds over $100 billion in Treasury bills. This is digital currency infrastructure built on the same monetary system Bitcoin was designed to replace.

From the perspective of Bitcoin's addressable market, every dollar in a fiat stablecoin is a dollar that did not enter Bitcoin. ARK Invest formalized this observation in November 2025, reducing its 2030 bull-case Bitcoin price target by $300,000 — specifically because stablecoins are capturing the emerging-market demand ARK had previously assigned to Bitcoin. Monthly stablecoin transaction volumes approached $970 billion by August 2025. In Latin America, 71% of stablecoin activity is tied to cross-border payments. In sub-Saharan Africa and Southeast Asia, stablecoins are functioning as dollar-access infrastructure for populations with limited banking services.

These are the populations that should be using Bitcoin. They are using dollar-denominated tokens instead. The stablecoin market is capturing the specific use case — peer-to-peer digital payments for the unbanked and underbanked — that Satoshi Nakamoto described in the first sentence of the Bitcoin white paper.

The stablecoin market has not stolen Bitcoin's future. It has temporarily borrowed it — and it can be reclaimed by any instrument that solves the same problem using Bitcoin as the reserve asset.

2. The Foundation: BTCADP

Both products depend on a reference price that no single institution controls. The Bitcoin Average Daily Price (BTCADP) is an open-source specification for computing a daily Bitcoin reference price from trade data across qualifying exchanges. It uses per-exchange volume-weighted average prices aggregated via a 25% trimmed mean with equal exchange weighting, covering the UTC midnight-to-midnight window. The full specification is published at btcadp.org.

The design principle is institutional independence: the methodology is the authority, not the publisher. Any party with the specification and trade data can independently reproduce the number. No institution owns it. It cannot be captured, manipulated through volume-weighting, or discontinued by any single entity.

The BTCADP underpins the ₿C unit of account — defined as the arithmetic mean of every historical BTCADP value from the genesis block through the previous completed day. As of early 2026, this cumulative average incorporates over 6,200 daily price observations and sits at approximately $18,700. This denomination — not Bitcoin's spot price — is what the ₿ond product is denominated in.

3. Three Products, Three Audiences

The ecosystem comprises three financial instruments with distinct mechanics, distinct user bases, and distinct roles. ₿USD captures transactional flow. The ₿ond captures long-term savings capital. ₿ILL captures institutional capital markets demand. Each makes the others more valuable, and every instrument issued mechanically requires Bitcoin to be purchased and held in reserve.

₿OND — Bitcoin Currency Savings

A return-based savings instrument denominated in ₿C. A saver deposits fiat — any amount from $1 upward — and selects a target return: +10%, +25%, +50%, +100%, or other tiers defined by the consortium. The bond matures when two conditions are simultaneously met: the saver's ₿C return target has been reached, and the treasury's BTC position on that specific bond is profitable. At maturity, the saver receives their original deposit plus the target return in ₿USD.

Purpose: Long-term savings with a saver-chosen return, fully backed by Bitcoin on the base layer. The return is fixed. The timeline is variable.

₿USD — Treasury-Backed Digital Currency

Maintains a hard peg of exactly $1.00. When a user purchases ₿USD, the $1 in fiat is used to purchase Bitcoin at spot, deposited into the ₿USD reserve ledger. Everything between issuance and redemption — all commerce, payments, and peer-to-peer transfers — is token circulation. No Bitcoin moves during circulation. Reserves sit on Bitcoin's base layer in transparent, publicly auditable on-chain wallets.

Purpose: A stable, spendable digital currency for everyday commerce — always worth exactly $1, backed by Bitcoin rather than government debt.

₿ILL — Capital Markets Instrument

A tradable, fungible capital markets instrument designed for institutional investors. ₿ILL shares the same architectural foundation as the ₿ond — ₿C denomination, dual-condition maturity, Bitcoin reserve backing, consortium issuance — but is tradable on secondary markets and issued in standardized tranches. The relationship mirrors I-Bonds (₿ond) and TIPS (₿ILL) in traditional finance.

Purpose: Institutional yield with secondary market liquidity — producing a Bitcoin-backed yield curve that portfolio managers can screen alongside T-bills and corporate bonds.

The retail products connect through conversion. A ₿ond holder who wants to spend converts their ₿C balance into ₿USD tokens at the current ₿C/USD rate. A ₿USD holder who wants to save converts tokens into a ₿ond. Every conversion between the retail products keeps capital inside the Bitcoin economy. ₿ILL operates on its own secondary market, with its own dedicated two-ledger reserve system entirely separate from ₿USD and ₿ond reserves. The only exit that costs Bitcoin is redemption to fiat.

4. The Six-Ledger Reserve Architecture

Each instrument in the framework has structurally different redemption mechanics, duration profiles, and risk characteristics. ₿USD faces demand-deposit risk. ₿ond faces maturity-timing risk. ₿ILL faces secondary-market pricing risk. These cannot share a reserve pool. The architectural principle is universal: every product has its own dedicated two-ledger system.

Ledger Product Contents Function
₿ond L1 ₿ond (savings) BTC purchased with incoming fiat at ₿ond issuance. Held for the full maturity period. Backs each ₿ond's ₿C-denominated obligation at maturity. Locked to maturity schedule.
₿ond L2 ₿ond (backstop) Additional BTC from treasury companies' existing holdings. Sized actuarially against the known maturity book. Covers redemption shortfalls where BTC spot at maturity falls below the ₿C obligation value.
₿USD L1 ₿USD (transactional) BTC purchased with incoming fiat at $1 per token. Grows and shrinks with circulating ₿USD supply. Backs every outstanding ₿USD token 1:1 in fiat terms at issuance. BTC appreciation creates surplus that fortifies L2.
₿USD L2 ₿USD (backstop) Additional BTC from treasury holdings. BTC appreciation surplus from L1 flows here over time. Covers redemption shortfalls where BTC spot has declined below $1 per token equivalent. Self-fortifies over time.
₿ILL L1 ₿ILL (institutional) BTC purchased with institutional capital at issuance. Parameters defined by consortium at each tranche issuance. Backs ₿ILL obligations. Sized against the institutional obligation book for each tranche.
₿ILL L2 ₿ILL (backstop) Additional BTC from treasury holdings. Follows same backstop logic as other products. Covers shortfalls specific to ₿ILL's tradable, secondary-market pricing dynamics.
The ₿USD L2 self-fortification mechanism

Every ₿USD token is minted at $1 backed by BTC purchased at spot. As Bitcoin's spot price appreciates, the BTC backing each $1 token becomes worth more than $1. That surplus flows into ₿USD L2 automatically. The reserve system does not need to be recapitalized from external sources as long as Bitcoin's long-run price trend continues. It recapitalizes itself.

5. Structural Defenses: Dual Condition and Defensive Programmability

The ₿OND's dual-condition maturity is the primary structural defense of the savings product. Each bond matures only when both the saver's ₿C return target is met and the treasury's BTC position on that specific bond is profitable. The treasury never pays out at a loss — this is not a promise but the maturity trigger itself. Early exits convert to ₿USD rather than drawing on ₿OND reserves, preventing coordinated mass redemptions from reaching the savings reserve base.

For ₿USD, the defense against coordinated attacks operates through defensive programmability. Every ₿USD token carries provenance metadata about itself — mint date, mint-day BTC spot price, block age, and transfer count — but cannot identify its holder. The defensive toolkit includes: BTC-default redemption (consortium transfers Bitcoin directly instead of selling on market), time-weighted fees (freshly minted tokens pay higher fees, making rapid mint-and-redeem attacks expensive), volume-triggered escalation (automatic circuit breakers on mass fiat redemptions), and ₿OND conversion (holders move from spending to savings layer during stress, contracting supply without any reserve action).

Consider a coordinated short-plus-redemption attack against ₿USD. BTC-default redemption produces no spot impact and no short profit. Time-weighted fees on freshly minted tokens cost 3–6% before any potential profit. Volume-triggered escalation adds further friction at scale. The expected return of such an attack is deeply negative. The mechanisms do not eliminate the theoretical possibility of attack. They make it economically irrational to attempt.

Because ₿OND obligations are denominated in ₿C and BTC spot has historically appreciated faster than the cumulative average, ₿OND L2 can be sized actuarially against the known maturity book with a structural tendency toward overcollateralization that strengthens over time.

6. The Flywheel: A Self-Reinforcing Demand Engine

The demand flywheel that activates when both products reach scale is structural, not speculative. Each component reinforces the others.

The ₿ond / ₿USD Flywheel
①New savers purchase ₿onds — fiat locked into BTC reserves for the full maturity period
▼
②New transactors mint ₿USD — each $1 minted requires $1 of BTC purchased at spot
▼
③Combined inflows create sustained, structural buying pressure on Bitcoin
▼
④BTC appreciation deepens reserve surplus — both ₿USD L2 and ₿ond spread widen
▼
⑤Deeper reserves lower systemic risk — the system becomes more secure as it grows
▼
⑥Lower risk and proven stability attract more savers, transactors, and merchants
▼
⑦More participants mean more ₿ond issuance and more ₿USD minting — return to ①

Every ₿ond issued locks capital into Bitcoin for years. Every ₿USD minted requires Bitcoin to be purchased today. The ecosystem does not borrow from Bitcoin's future. It builds it.

8. The Treasury Company Opportunity

Bitcoin treasury companies are not being asked to take a new risk. They have already taken the risk. Every company in this cohort has committed its balance sheet to the thesis that Bitcoin will appreciate over time. The ₿ond/₿USD architecture does not add to that risk. It creates revenue-generating products on top of an existing risk position — and converts open-ended price risk into a defined, actuarially manageable one.

Revenue streams include: issuance fees on ₿ONDs and ₿USD; redemption fees tiered by exit path (near-zero for BTC-default, highest for fiat exit); the ₿OND maturity spread — the primary long-term revenue engine, where BTC spot appreciation outpaces the ₿C-denominated obligation and the dual condition captures that surplus at every payout; ₿USD reserve appreciation as BTC surplus flows into L2; and an equity re-rating premium as markets assign higher multiples to a business with recurring revenue, a growing user base, and structural alignment between industry growth and primary asset appreciation.

The consortium does not promise to maintain the value of the currency. The mathematics does. That distinction is the entire argument.

11. Timing

Three conditions had to develop independently before the ₿ond/₿USD model became viable. All three have now been met simultaneously for the first time.

₿C denomination stability. As of early 2026, with over 6,200 days of history, the sensitivity of ₿C to any single day's price movement is below 0.01%. There is no shortcut to this accumulation. That time has elapsed.

Bitcoin treasury company scale. The combined Bitcoin holdings of public treasury companies now exceed one million BTC. This reserve base, organized into a consortium, would constitute one of the largest Bitcoin custodians in existence.

Competitive timing. Over 130 countries are in some stage of CBDC exploration or rollout. The window in which a Bitcoin-native alternative can be established before sovereign digital currencies become the default retail payment infrastructure is open now and will not remain open indefinitely.

12. Risk Analysis

Bitcoin price decline. A sustained, severe Bitcoin price decline is the most acute risk to both products. The critical framing: every consortium member already carries this risk on their primary balance sheet. The reserve commitments formalize existing exposure with defined perimeters and attach revenue streams to it.

Bank run and coordinated attack. For the ₿OND, the dual-condition maturity ensures the treasury never pays out at a loss, and early exits convert to ₿USD rather than drawing on ₿OND reserves. For ₿USD, defenses include BTC-default redemption (no market selling), time-weighted fees on freshly minted tokens, volume-triggered escalation, on-chain reserve transparency, and the L2 backstop self-fortified by BTC appreciation.

Regulatory uncertainty. The structural mitigation is the consortium's distributed jurisdiction — no single regulatory action shuts down the entire system. The ₿C denomination itself requires no permission to compute or use.

Consortium coordination. The foundational methodology is independent of the consortium — the BTCADP specification is published, the ₿C formula is arithmetic, and reserve management rules can be codified in a charter requiring no active committee decisions for routine operations.

Conclusion

Bitcoin solved the problem of digital scarcity. What it did not solve was the presentation problem — how to offer the stability that commerce and savings require without abandoning the reserve asset that makes Bitcoin's monetary properties unique.

The ₿ond/₿USD/₿ILL ecosystem solves the presentation problem with three instruments that serve distinct purposes and reinforce each other. The six-ledger reserve architecture keeps the three products cleanly separated so that stress on one cannot contaminate the others. The fixed maturity structure eliminates the bank run attack vector. The fee hierarchy routes capital toward paths that keep it inside the Bitcoin economy.

The components exist. The BTCADP specification is published. The ₿C denomination is mathematically defined. The treasury companies that would form the consortium already hold over one million Bitcoin in aggregate. What remains is assembly.

The central banks of the digital Bitcoin economy have not yet been built. The institutions that build them will define how value moves globally for the next century.
