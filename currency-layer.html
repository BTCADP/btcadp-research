Bitcoin Currency Layer

A four-component monetary system serving two spheres simultaneously — meeting the fiat world where it is, and building the Bitcoin circular economy where it needs to go.

01

The Problem

Bitcoin has operated without interruption since January 2009. Its network works. Its supply is fixed. Its settlement is final. By every technical measure, it is the hardest money ever created.

And yet Bitcoin has not achieved widespread adoption as everyday money. The barrier is not technical. It is an interface problem: Bitcoin's spot price is volatile, making it impractical for the pricing, invoicing, and daily commerce that constitute most economic activity. A merchant cannot price a sandwich in BTC today and have that price mean the same thing tomorrow. A worker cannot plan a budget around a salary that swings 10% in a week.

The conventional response — fiat-pegged stablecoins — solves volatility by abandoning Bitcoin entirely. Every dollar that flows into USDT or USDC goes into US Treasury bills, not Bitcoin. The $317 billion stablecoin market has become one of the largest purchasers of US government debt on earth, capturing the exact demand that Bitcoin was designed to serve.

The problem was never Bitcoin. The problem was the absence of a denomination stable enough for daily commerce and an interface familiar enough for the fiat majority. The currency layer provides both — without abandoning Bitcoin, without a fiat peg on the reserve asset, and without requiring any change to the Bitcoin protocol.
02

Two Spheres

The Bitcoin community has long held two coherent visions of how Bitcoin succeeds — those building toward a Bitcoin circular economy where no fiat reference is needed, and those accumulating Bitcoin as the apex reserve asset through institutional channels. Both visions share the same destination. They disagree about the path.

The currency layer dissolves that disagreement. It serves both visions through a single system operating in two spheres simultaneously.

Sphere A

The Fiat Economy

Most of the world lives here. People earn in dollars, spend in dollars, and measure value in dollars. The currency layer meets them where they are: a $1 stablecoin they can spend (₿USD) and a savings product that grows rather than erodes (the ₿ond). They experience Bitcoin's monetary properties through familiar instruments — without needing to understand Bitcoin's vocabulary, consult the spot price, or manage satoshis.

Sphere B

The Bitcoin Economy

The Bitcoin circular economy — the original vision of the white paper. Prices are denominated in ₿C. Wages are paid in ₿C. Savings are held in ₿C. No fiat reference is consulted because none is needed. The currency layer serves this sphere through ₿C: a Bitcoin-native unit of account stable enough for daily commerce, appreciating in purchasing power over time, requiring no issuer, no reserves, and no trusted third party.

same denomination  ·  same formula  ·  same Bitcoin backing

Sphere A is the on-ramp to Sphere B. A participant enters through a familiar dollar-denominated interface, accumulates ₿C-denominated value, and gradually finds less reason to exit to fiat as the Bitcoin economy deepens around them. The transition is voluntary, gradual, and driven by economic incentive — not ideology.

The Two Spheres framework examines this model in full — how ₿C operates in both contexts simultaneously, and how Sphere A becomes the gateway to the Bitcoin circular economy that both camps have always been building toward. Read: Two Spheres →
03

Four Components

The currency layer consists of four components, each independent, each performing a specific function. Each layer depends only on the layer below it — none requires the layer above it. A failure at any layer does not propagate downward.

Layer 1
01
BTCADP — The Reference Price

An open-source specification for computing a single, reproducible daily Bitcoin reference price. The methodology is the authority — no institution is required. Computed via a 25% trimmed mean of qualifying exchange VWAPs with equal exchange weighting. Any party with the specification and trade data can reproduce the number independently. If btcadp.org ceased to exist, the standard continues.

Layer 2
02
BTCC (₿C) — The Unit of Account

The cumulative arithmetic mean of every historical BTCADP value from the genesis block through the previous completed day. Not a token. Not a stablecoin. A denomination — derived entirely from Bitcoin's own price history. Moves approximately 0.04% per day. Appreciates in purchasing power over time. No issuer. No reserves. Pure mathematics. Operates identically in Sphere A (fiat-expressible for reference) and Sphere B (native unit of account for the Bitcoin circular economy).

Layer 3
03
TBDC / ₿USD — The Medium of Exchange

Treasury-Backed Digital Currency (TBDC) is the instrument class. ₿USD is the US dollar implementation: one token equals exactly $1.00, always redeemable for $1.00. Issued by a consortium of Bitcoin treasury companies. Every $1 minted requires $1 of Bitcoin purchased at spot and deposited into publicly auditable on-chain wallets. The Sphere A spending instrument — a dollar that meets users where they are, backed by Bitcoin rather than government debt.

Layer 4
04
₿ond — The Savings Product

A fixed-maturity savings instrument denominated in BTCC (₿C), issued by a Bitcoin treasury company. A saver deposits fiat. The treasury purchases Bitcoin at spot and holds it in reserve. The saver holds a ₿C-denominated balance until maturity. At maturity the obligation is returned as ₿USD — keeping value inside the Bitcoin ecosystem. The ₿ond is where ₿C's long-run appreciation is captured by long-term savers. Its fixed maturity structure eliminates the bank run attack vector entirely.

If every ₿USD issuer ceased operations, ₿C would still be computable. If ₿C were abandoned, BTCADP would remain a valid reference price. Each component can be evaluated, adopted, or rejected independently. The stack is modular. The destination is singular.

How the Layer Works

The architecture maintains a clean separation between the interface users experience and the Bitcoin reserves that back it. Token circulation on the payment layer never touches the base layer reserves.

05

TBDC vs. CBDC

Central Bank Digital Currencies are issued by entities with legal authority to expand the money supply. A Treasury-Backed Digital Currency is the structural inverse — every token requires actual Bitcoin purchased at market price. The supply cannot be inflated. No governance vote changes this. It is a property of the reserve asset.

PropertyCBDCTBDC / ₿USD
Reserve asset Government bonds, foreign reserves, discretionary assets Bitcoin only — fixed supply, on-chain, auditable in real time
Issuance constraint At the discretion of a central bank committee. No supply ceiling. Every token requires Bitcoin purchased at market price. Cannot be inflated.
Monetary policy Controlled by committee. Rates, quantitative easing, open market operations. None. Bitcoin's supply is fixed by protocol. No committee. No discretion.
Reserve transparency Periodic reports. Limited public visibility. Trust the institution. All four reserve ledgers on-chain. Verifiable by anyone, in real time, without an auditor.
Censorship Accounts can be frozen by the issuer or government order. Base layer custody on Bitcoin. No single entity can unilaterally freeze reserves.
Single point of failure Sovereign authority. Political capture. Systemic regulatory risk. Multiple independent companies across jurisdictions. No single point of failure.
06

The Flywheel

Every ₿USD token minted requires the consortium to purchase Bitcoin at spot. Every ₿ond issued locks Bitcoin in reserve for the full maturity period. The demand these products generate for Bitcoin is structural — tied to real economic activity, not speculation — and does not reverse when market sentiment shifts.

01 — Minting creates demand
Every ₿USD minted buys Bitcoin

Every $1 of ₿USD issued requires $1 of Bitcoin purchased at spot. Every ₿ond issued locks that Bitcoin in reserve for years. Commerce-driven demand does not reverse when sentiment shifts.

02 — Appreciation deepens reserves
BTC appreciation fortifies the backstop

As Bitcoin's spot price rises, reserves in ₿USD Ledger 1 exceed the $1 obligation per token. That surplus flows into Ledger 2 — the backstop deepens without requiring additional capital.

03 — Scale reduces risk
The system gets safer as it grows

As more commerce occurs natively in ₿USD and ₿C, the proportion of tokens redeemed for fiat declines. Reserves accumulate appreciation without outflow — the structural inverse of fractional reserve banking.

04 — The gateway opens
Sphere A becomes Sphere B

As participants accumulate ₿C-denominated value, the need for fiat reference diminishes naturally. The Bitcoin economy deepens. Sphere A participants become Sphere B participants — by preference, not by mandate.

Every new ₿USD user is a Bitcoin user. Every ₿ond issued is a Bitcoin purchase locked for years. The currency layer does not compete with Bitcoin. It is the mechanism by which Bitcoin fulfills its original purpose — peer-to-peer electronic cash — for the billions of people the stablecoin market has already demonstrated are ready to use it.
