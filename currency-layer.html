Bitcoin Currency Layer

A system in which everyday users transact in a stable denomination — displayed in dollars — while the underlying reserves are held entirely in Bitcoin. The volatility that has prevented Bitcoin from functioning as everyday money is abstracted away at the interface level.

01

The Problem

Bitcoin's failure to achieve widespread adoption as a medium of everyday exchange is not a failure of its network or security model. The protocol has operated continuously since January 2009 without interruption. The failure is at the interface layer: the way Bitcoin's value is presented to users.

A worker paid in BTC cannot predict next month's purchasing power. A merchant who prices goods in BTC must reprice constantly. A business holding BTC receivables faces balance sheet volatility that complicates accounting and financial planning. The conventional response — fiat-pegged stablecoins — solves volatility by abandoning Bitcoin entirely. The cure eliminates the disease by killing the patient.

A different approach is possible. Rather than replacing Bitcoin with a fiat proxy, a currency layer wraps Bitcoin in an interface that removes the end user from spot price volatility entirely. The underlying reserve asset remains Bitcoin. The abstraction is complete: the user's experience of stability is real, the Bitcoin backing is real, and at no point does the user need to reconcile the two.
02

Three Components

The currency layer consists of three distinct components, each independent, each performing a specific function. Each layer depends only on the layer below it — none requires the layer above it.

Layer 1
BTCADP

The reference price. An open-source daily Bitcoin price computed from qualifying exchanges using a 25% trimmed mean. Any party can reproduce it independently. The methodology is the authority — no institution required.

Layer 2
Bitcoin Currency (₿C)

The unit of account. Cumulative arithmetic mean of every historical BTCADP since genesis. Moves approximately 0.04% per day. Appreciates over time. No issuer. No reserves. Pure mathematics. The denomination in which prices, wages, and savings are expressed across both Sphere A and Sphere B.

Layer 3
₿USD

The medium of exchange. Treasury-Backed Digital Currency (TBDC) is the instrument class; ₿USD is the US dollar implementation. One token equals one US dollar. Issued by a consortium of Bitcoin treasury companies. Backed by Bitcoin in a four-ledger reserve architecture. Redeemable for fiat at any time.

Layer 4
₿ond

The savings product. A fixed-maturity instrument denominated in BTCC (₿C). Savers deposit fiat, the treasury purchases Bitcoin at spot, and the saver holds a ₿C-denominated balance until maturity. At maturity the obligation is returned as ₿USD, keeping value inside the Bitcoin economy. The ₿ond captures ₿C's long-run appreciation for long-term savers.

A failure or disruption at any layer does not propagate downward. If every ₿USD issuer ceased operations, ₿C would still be computable. If ₿C were abandoned, BTCADP would remain a valid reference price. Each component can be evaluated, adopted, or rejected independently.

03

The Architecture

The architecture maintains a clean separation between two layers that operate independently. Token circulation on the sidechain never touches the Bitcoin reserves on the base layer.

A user who mints ₿USD tokens with fiat, spends them at merchants, receives them as salary, saves them for months, and eventually redeems some for fiat has interacted with Bitcoin's base layer exactly twice — at the beginning and at the end. Everything in between was sidechain commerce, denominated in dollars, displayed in their native currency. Bitcoin's spot volatility was present throughout — the user never encountered it.

05

₿USD vs. CBDC

The term ₿USD is deliberate. Central Bank Digital Currencies are issued by entities with the legal authority to expand the money supply. A Treasury-Backed Digital Currency is the structural inverse.

Property CBDC ₿USD
Issuance At discretion of central bank. No supply constraint. Only against Bitcoin reserves acquired at market price.
Monetary Policy Controlled by committee. Rates, QE, open market ops. ₿USD issuance is capped by the ₿C floor. Cannot be inflated beyond Bitcoin reserves.
Purchasing Power Erodes over time by design (inflation targeting). $1.00 peg for the token. Underlying Bitcoin reserves appreciate over time.
Reserves Discretionary. Government bonds, foreign currencies. Bitcoin only. Verifiable on-chain in real time.
Transparency Periodic reports. Limited public visibility. Reserves on-chain. Methodology open-source. Token supply auditable.
Single Point of Failure Yes. Sovereign authority, political capture. No. Multiple independent companies across jurisdictions.
The most consequential difference: a ₿USD consortium cannot inflate the money supply. This is not a policy commitment that could be reversed under pressure. It is an immutable property of the reserve asset. No governance vote, no emergency measure, no executive decision can produce additional Bitcoin.
06

The Virtuous Cycle

Every ₿USD token minted requires the consortium to purchase actual Bitcoin on the open market. There is no synthetic exposure. One hundred percent of demand for ₿USD tokens flows directly to BTC spot as buying pressure. This creates a self-reinforcing cycle.

01
Minting creates Bitcoin demand

Every ₿USD token minted requires purchasing Bitcoin at spot. Commerce-driven demand is persistent — it does not reverse when sentiment shifts.

02
Demand supports spot price

Sustained structural buying from ₿USD minting supports BTC spot. Unlike speculative demand, this is tied to real economic activity.

03
Rising spot widens treasury surplus

As BTC spot rises, the gap between Ledger 1 asset values and ₿USD liabilities widens. Treasury companies accumulate surplus. Reserves deepen.

04
Growth reduces redemption pressure

As the ₿USD economy matures, users earn, spend, and save in ₿USD without needing fiat. The system's risk decreases precisely as it scales.

Scale makes the system safer, not more fragile — the structural inverse of traditional banking. Every user who enters the ₿USD economy is a Bitcoin user. Every transaction settles on Bitcoin infrastructure. The currency layer does not compete with Bitcoin. It fulfills Bitcoin's original purpose.

The philosophical framework behind the currency layer

The currency layer serves two spheres: the fiat economy (Sphere A) and the Bitcoin circular economy (Sphere B). The components described here — BTCADP, ₿C, TBDC/₿USD, and the ₿ond — operate across both. Understanding the two-sphere model is understanding why the currency layer is built the way it is.
